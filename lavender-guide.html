How to Grow Lavender for Cocktails & Mocktails

Lavender brings floral elegance and calming aromatics to garden-to-glass living. Learn how to grow, harvest, preserve, and use it in syrups, cocktails, mocktails, teas, and seasonal creations.

Why Grow Lavender?

Most herbs are workhorses. Lavender is something different — it's the herb that transforms a drink from good to memorable. One small sprig in the right glass, one careful syrup, and the whole character of a cocktail shifts. Here's why it earns its place in a bar garden. For the full picture on cocktail herbs, see the Cocktail Herb Guide.

✨
Instantly memorable
No other herb stops people mid-sip quite like lavender. Used with restraint it adds a floral complexity that makes drinks feel considered and special — the kind of thing guests ask about without being able to place it. That's exactly where you want to be with a garnish or a syrup.
🌸
A season worth waiting for
Lavender's short bloom window — three to four weeks in high summer — makes the harvest feel earned. There's something in that seasonality that tastes better in the glass. Drinks made with ingredients that have a moment are drinks worth making.
🏺
Dries beautifully
Unlike most fresh herbs, lavender gets better when dried. The fragrance concentrates, the flavour deepens, and a bunch hung in the kitchen gives you a year-round supply. One good harvest in July means lavender syrups, garnishes, and infusions right through to the following spring.
☀️
Low maintenance once established
Lavender thrives on neglect. Once it's settled into a sunny, well-drained spot, it asks for almost nothing — no regular feeding, minimal watering, no fussing. The main task is an annual prune after flowering to keep it compact and productive. That's it.
🫗
Exceptional in mocktails
Lavender is one of the best herbs for alcohol-free drinks because it contributes the kind of floral complexity that a spirit usually provides. Lavender lemonade, lavender honey soda, and lavender sparkling water are drinks that feel like an occasion even without the gin. Browse our full recipe collection.
🌿
Works across the whole garden
Lavender is at home in a formal border, a container on a sunny terrace, or a kitchen window box. It's as happy growing next to rosemary and thyme as it is in a dedicated herb bed. Plant it where you'll brush against it — that moment of releasing fragrance as you pass is one of the small pleasures of a bar garden.

Choose your plant

Which Lavender Variety is Best for Drinks?

Not all lavender tastes the same in a glass. Species, variety, and growing conditions all affect the flavour — and two of the most popular lavenders in garden centres are essentially unusable for cocktails. Choose carefully from the start.

Hidcote
Best for cocktails
Type
English lavender (Lavandula angustifolia)
Flavour
Deeply floral, clean, and sweet with almost no camphor. The most refined and bar-friendly lavender available.
Why it works
Compact habit, abundant flower spikes, and the highest culinary oil quality of any commonly available cultivar.
Best used for
Syrups, garnishes, infusions, drying
Vera
Excellent choice
Type
English lavender (Lavandula angustifolia)
Flavour
Floral and slightly sweet, with longer flower spikes than Hidcote. Excellent fragrance, good culinary quality.
Why it works
Larger flower heads give more to harvest per stem. Popular in culinary lavender production for exactly this reason.
Best used for
Drying, syrups, sugar infusions
Munstead
Good option
Type
English lavender (Lavandula angustifolia)
Flavour
Slightly lighter than Hidcote, still clean and floral. Less intense — which can be an advantage in delicate drinks.
Why it works
A reliable, widely available English lavender. Good for those who want a subtler floral note rather than a pronounced one.
Best used for
Garnish, light syrups, sugar rims
French & Spanish Lavender
Avoid for drinks
Type
Lavandula dentata / Lavandula stoechas
Flavour
Strong camphor and medicinal notes that overpower rather than enhance. More like lavender polish than lavender field.
Why to avoid
Both are beautiful garden plants but the higher camphor content makes them unpleasant in food and drink. Common in garden centres — always check the label.
Use instead
Any Lavandula angustifolia cultivar

In the ground

How to Grow Lavender

Lavender has two requirements that are genuinely non-negotiable: full sun and excellent drainage. Get those right and almost everything else follows. Get them wrong and the plant will struggle no matter what else you do.

What lavender needs

  • Sun: Minimum 6 hours of direct sunlight per day, 8 hours is better. In anything less, it grows but doesn't bloom well.
  • Drainage: The single most important factor. Lavender originated on rocky Mediterranean hillsides — it cannot tolerate wet feet. Amend heavy soil with sharp sand or horticultural grit before planting.
  • Soil pH: Slightly alkaline (pH 6.5 to 7.5). If your soil is acidic, a light dusting of garden lime helps.
  • Watering: Sparse. Water new plants regularly until established (first 6 to 8 weeks), then let the plant dry out between waterings. Established lavender in the ground rarely needs watering at all except in drought.
  • Feeding: Go light. Excess nitrogen encourages soft, leafy growth at the expense of flowers and fragrance. A low-nitrogen feed in early spring is all it needs.
  • Spacing: Plant 18–24 inches apart to allow air circulation. Good airflow reduces the fungal issues that wet, crowded plants attract.

Common growing mistakes

  • Planting in heavy, wet soil
    Root rot is the number one lavender killer. If your soil holds water, the plant will die slowly regardless of everything else you do right. Either raise the bed, add significant grit, or grow in a container.
  • Planting in partial shade
    That shady corner might look like enough light, but lavender without full sun produces fewer flowers and weaker fragrance. Both matter enormously when you're harvesting for the bar.
  • Cutting into old wood
    The single most common pruning mistake. Lavender does not regenerate from the grey-brown woody base. Always cut back into green growth only — if you go too far, you won't get new shoots and the plant dies.
  • Skipping the annual prune
    An unpruned lavender becomes an open, woody, leggy shrub that flowers poorly. Cut back by a third each year after flowering to keep it compact, vigorous, and productive for the bar garden.
Growing in a container

Lavender does very well in pots — particularly in wetter climates where drainage is hard to guarantee in the ground. Use a terracotta container (it breathes, unlike plastic), add 25 to 30% perlite to potting mix, and position in the sunniest spot available. Water when the top two inches of soil are dry. Repot every two to three years in spring before new growth begins.

The moment matters

When and How to Harvest

Timing your lavender harvest correctly makes a significant difference to the flavour and fragrance of everything you make with it. Too early and the oils aren't fully developed. Too late and the flowers begin to shed and lose potency.

Step 01

Watch the buds, not the calendar

Harvest when the buds on the lower third of each spike are just beginning to open — you'll see the first colour breaking but the majority are still closed. This is when aromatic oil content is at its peak. A fully open flower spike looks beautiful but has already passed its fragrance prime.

Step 02

Cut in the morning

Essential oils are most concentrated in the morning, after any dew has dried but before the midday heat begins to volatilise them. This applies to fresh use and to harvesting for drying — morning lavender is noticeably more fragrant than afternoon lavender.

Step 03

Cut long stems, into green growth only

Use clean, sharp secateurs. Cut stems as long as possible for drying — it makes bundling easier and gives you more flexibility. The critical rule: always cut into green, leafy growth. Never cut below the point where leaves appear on the stem. Bare wood will not regenerate.

Step 04

Harvest generously — it benefits the plant

Unlike cutting into old wood, harvesting flower spikes is good for the plant. Removing spent or nearly-spent flower heads prevents the plant from putting energy into seed production and encourages it to produce a second flush later in the season. Don't be precious about it.

Step 05

Use fresh immediately or begin drying straight away

Fresh lavender wilts quickly once cut. Use it the same day if you're making a syrup or using it as a fresh garnish. For everything else, move directly to drying — don't leave cut stems in water like you would cut flowers, as this can encourage mildew before they dry properly.

Step 06

Prune after the main harvest

Once you've taken your main harvest, use it as your annual pruning moment. Cut the whole plant back by about a third into the green growth, shaping it into a tidy mound. This is what keeps lavender productive and compact year after year. Don't leave it until fall — the plant needs time to harden off before winter.

Make it last

Preserving Your Lavender Harvest

Lavender's short bloom window is actually its most useful quality for the bar — once dried, it keeps for up to a year with virtually no loss in flavour. One good harvest in July can supply syrups, garnishes, and infusions through the following spring.

🌾
Drying
Bundle 8 to 10 stems together, secure with a rubber band (it tightens as stems shrink), and hang upside-down in a warm, dark, well-ventilated space. Ready in 1 to 2 weeks. Whole dried stems are beautiful as garnishes; strip the buds into an airtight jar for making syrups.
Store dried buds in a glass jar away from heat and light. Quality holds for up to 12 months.
🫙
Lavender Simple Syrup
Combine 1 cup sugar and 1 cup water in a saucepan over medium heat. Once sugar dissolves, remove from heat, add 1 to 2 tablespoons dried lavender buds, and steep for 10 to 15 minutes. Taste frequently — lavender moves fast. Strain, cool, and refrigerate in a sealed jar.
Keeps for 3 to 4 weeks refrigerated. Freeze in ice cube trays for up to 6 months.
🍯
Lavender Sugar
Layer dried lavender buds with white or caster sugar in a jar, seal, and leave for 1 to 2 weeks. The sugar absorbs the fragrance. Use for rimming cocktail glasses, sweetening sparkling water, or anywhere you want a whisper of lavender without steeping.
Lavender sugar keeps indefinitely. A small jar makes an excellent gift from the garden.
🧊
Syrup Ice Cubes
Pour finished lavender syrup into ice cube trays and freeze. Each cube is roughly 1 oz — exactly the right amount for a single cocktail. Drop a cube directly into a shaker or glass and it dilutes as it should while delivering consistent flavour all year.
Keeps frozen for up to 6 months. Label the tray so you don't confuse it with mint syrup cubes.
🫗
Spirit Infusion
Add 1 tablespoon dried lavender buds to 250ml of vodka or gin. Taste every 30 minutes — lavender infuses quickly and bitterness develops fast. Most infusions are done in 1 to 3 hours. Strain through a fine mesh sieve, bottle, and label. Use within 3 months.
Gin infusions are especially good. The botanical overlap creates something that tastes like gin was always meant to contain lavender.
🌿
Dried Garnishes
Whole dried lavender stems make exceptional cocktail garnishes that hold their structure and fragrance long after the flower season is over. Keep a bunch tied with twine in the bar area. A single stem laid across the rim of a glass is one of the simplest and most effective cocktail presentations you can produce from the garden.
Select stems with the fullest, most symmetrical flower spikes for garnish duty. Tie them tightly so they dry straight.

At the bar

Using Lavender in Drinks

Lavender rewards restraint more than any other cocktail herb. The line between elegant and overwhelming is a short one — but stay on the right side of it and you have an ingredient that elevates almost everything it touches.

🫙
Simple Syrup
The most versatile lavender preparation for the bar. A well-made lavender syrup can go into gin sours, lemonades, sparkling wine cocktails, and mocktails. Use 1 to 2 tablespoons of dried buds per cup of sugar-water, steep for 10 to 15 minutes off the heat, and strain thoroughly.
Taste every 5 minutes during steeping. Over-steeped lavender syrup tastes medicinal. When it smells like a field, it's done.
🌸
Garnish
A single dried or fresh lavender stem laid across the rim of a glass is one of the most beautiful cocktail garnishes available from a home garden. It adds fragrance that carries into each sip without contributing any actual flavour to the drink. The nose is doing most of the work.
Don't muddle or crush the garnish. Just lay it gently — the warmth of the glass will slowly release the fragrance.
🔥
Smoked Garnish
Briefly pass a dried lavender sprig through a flame, blow it out, and place it across the glass. The smoldering tip releases a plume of scented smoke as the drink is served. It's a technique that looks impressive and genuinely adds a smoky-floral dimension to the aromatic experience.
Use this on whisky sours, mezcal cocktails, or any drink where smoke would be welcome. Keep it brief — charred lavender smells unpleasant.
🍯
Lavender Sugar Rim
Combine lavender sugar with a little fine sea salt for a rim that adds sweetness, fragrance, and a subtle savoury note. Run a lemon wedge around the rim of the glass before dipping. Works especially well on gin sours and lavender lemonade cocktails.
The salt in the rim amplifies the lavender fragrance the same way salt heightens other flavours — don't skip it.
🧪
Infused Spirit
Lavender-infused gin or vodka lets you add floral character to a drink without relying on syrup or sugar. Use it as a direct swap for plain spirit, or combine with regular gin to dial in the intensity. Particularly good in martinis, spritzes, and anything served cold and clean.
Taste every 30 minutes. Once it smells like the inside of your grandmother's wardrobe, it's gone too far.
🫧
Lavender Honey
Gently warm honey and steep dried lavender buds in it for 20 minutes, then strain. The result is a rounded, floral sweetener that replaces plain honey or simple syrup in any recipe. It adds depth without sharpness — especially good in bee's knees variations, mead cocktails, and anything with lemon.
Keep a small jar at the bar. A spoonful stirred into sparkling water with a squeeze of lemon is one of the best alcohol-free garden drinks there is.

In the glass

What to Make with Garden Lavender

Lavender works across a wider range of drinks than most people expect. The key is knowing what it's friends with — lemon, honey, elderflower, and gin are its closest allies. Start with the Lavender Lemon Spritz and build from there.

Lavender Lemon Spritz
Cocktail · Mocktail · On the site
Lavender syrup, fresh lemon juice, and sparkling wine or sparkling water — built over ice and finished with a lavender sprig. The drink that belongs on a summer table as much as it belongs in a glass. Both the cocktail and the alcohol-free version are excellent.
Spirit: Gin or Elderflower Liqueur  ·  Mocktail: Sparkling water + elderflower cordial
Lavender Gin & Tonic
Cocktail · Garden Classic
Add a quarter ounce of lavender syrup to a standard gin and tonic and garnish with a fresh or dried lavender sprig. The syrup bridges the botanical gap between gin and tonic in a way that makes the drink taste like it was always supposed to be made this way. Use a dry, floral gin rather than a citrus-forward style.
Spirit: London Dry or Floral Gin  ·  Tonic: Classic or light tonic
Lavender Bee's Knees
Cocktail · Prohibition Classic
The Bee's Knees — gin, lemon juice, honey — is already close to perfect. Swap plain honey syrup for lavender honey and you get something that leans floral without losing the citrus backbone. Shake hard with ice and strain into a chilled coupe. A lavender sugar rim adds another layer.
Spirit: London Dry Gin  ·  Sweetener: Lavender honey syrup
Lavender Lemonade
Mocktail · Summer Staple
Fresh-squeezed lemon juice, lavender syrup, water, and ice. Adjust the ratio until the lavender is clearly present but not dominant — you want lemon leading, lavender following. Serve in a tall glass with plenty of ice and a long lavender sprig. One of the best alcohol-free things you can pour from the garden.
Add elderflower cordial for a more complex, floral finish
Lavender Vodka Sour
Cocktail · Easy Build
Vodka's neutrality makes it the perfect canvas for lavender — the floral note leads with nothing to compete. Shake vodka, lavender syrup, and fresh lemon juice with ice and strain over a large cube. A dried lavender sprig as garnish, and nothing else needed. Clean, elegant, and completely from the garden.
Spirit: Vodka  ·  Citrus: Fresh lemon only — no substitutes
Lavender Elderflower Spritz
Cocktail · Mocktail · Summer
Lavender and elderflower are one of the great floral pairings — different enough to be interesting, similar enough to be harmonious. Combine lavender syrup, elderflower liqueur (or cordial for a mocktail), fresh lemon, and sparkling wine or water over ice. A drink that tastes like the garden at its absolute peak.
Spirit: St. Germain or similar elderflower liqueur  ·  Mocktail: Elderflower cordial + sparkling water

Common Lavender Mistakes

Most lavender failures come down to the same handful of errors — and most of them are entirely avoidable. Here are the ones worth knowing about before you plant.

Mistake 01
Using the wrong variety
French and Spanish lavender are everywhere in garden centres and both are useless for cocktails. The camphor and resinous notes in these species make drinks taste medicinal. Always check the label: you want Lavandula angustifolia.
Fix: Choose Hidcote, Vera, or Munstead — all are English lavender cultivars suited to bar use.
Mistake 02
Planting in poor drainage
The plant will look fine for a while, then suddenly collapse. Root rot is silent and fast. Heavy clay soil or any site where water pools after rain is unsuitable without significant amendment.
Fix: Add a generous quantity of sharp sand or grit to the planting hole, or grow in a raised bed or container.
Mistake 03
Over-steeping the syrup
Lavender moves from elegant to medicinal very quickly during infusion. Many people follow recipe timings blindly — but lavender strength varies by variety, age, and how long it's been dried. The only reliable guide is taste.
Fix: Steep off the heat and taste every 5 minutes. Stop the moment it smells like a field rather than a pharmacy.
Mistake 04
Cutting into old wood
This is how lavender plants are killed by their owners rather than by the weather. The grey-brown woody base at the bottom of the plant is inert — it will not send out new shoots if cut back to that point. The plant simply dies.
Fix: Always leave green, leafy growth below your cut. If you can see leaves on the stem below where you're cutting, you're safe.
Mistake 05
Harvesting too late
A fully open flower spike looks like peak harvest — it isn't. The aromatic oils that make lavender worth growing for drinks are at their highest when the buds are just beginning to break. By the time the flowers are fully open, some of that potency is already gone.
Fix: Harvest when the lowest flowers on each spike are just starting to open. Watch the plant closely once you see colour breaking — the window is only a few days.
Mistake 06
Using too much in the drink
More is not more with lavender. A heavy hand produces drinks that smell like a spa and taste like they're trying too hard. The goal is for lavender to be felt rather than identified — something that adds a question mark of floral complexity without announcing itself.
Fix: Start with half the quantity you think you need. You can always add more syrup; you can't take it back out.

Lavender Growing FAQ

The questions that come up most often — answered plainly.

Lavender is straightforward once you understand its two non-negotiables: full sun and excellent drainage. Give it those two things and it's remarkably low-maintenance — drought-tolerant, rarely bothered by pests, and long-lived. The most common failure is wet soil, which causes root rot even in otherwise healthy plants. Get the drainage right and lavender will reward you for years.

Vera is another excellent choice with slightly larger flower spikes. Avoid French lavender (Lavandula dentata) and Spanish lavender (Lavandula stoechas), which have a stronger camphor quality that overpowers rather than elevates drinks.
