Mint · Garden Technique

How to Harvest &
Preserve Mint

Knowing when to cut, how to cut, and what to do with the excess is the difference between a mint plant that gives you one good season and one that keeps going for years. This is everything you need to know — from the first cut to the freezer.

Timing

When to Harvest Mint

Mint flavour isn't constant — it peaks at certain points in the plant's life and the day, and drops sharply when the plant flowers. Understanding this makes a noticeable difference in drink quality. For a full overview of mint growing, see the Mint Guide.

Time of Day

Harvest in the morning

Cut mint after any dew has dried but before the midday heat sets in — typically between 8 and 11am. Essential oil concentration is highest in the morning. These are the same volatile compounds responsible for mint's aroma and flavour in drinks. Afternoon heat causes partial evaporation of these oils before you've even picked the plant.

Plant Stage

Harvest just before flowering

The period just before a mint plant flowers is when flavour is at its absolute peak — the plant is concentrating all its energy into leaf production. Once flower buds appear, begin harvesting immediately and pinch off the buds as you go. A plant in full flower has already redirected energy away from the leaves, and the remaining leaves will start to turn bitter within days.

Frequency

Harvest every 2 to 3 weeks

Regular cutting is what keeps the plant productive. A mint plant left to its own devices will grow tall, flower, and stop putting energy into new leaf production. Frequent harvesting mimics natural grazing pressure and keeps the plant in a perpetual state of vegetative growth — which is exactly what you want for cocktail use. You can cut the whole plant back to 3 inches above the soil and it will regrow completely in 3 to 4 weeks.

Season

Spring through early fall

In most growing zones, mint is harvestable from when it emerges in spring until the first hard frost in fall. In warmer climates (zones 9 through 11), it may produce year-round with only a brief slow period in winter. The most productive months are June through September in most of North America. See the Planting Guide for zone-specific timing.

Technique

How to Harvest

The way you cut mint affects how the plant regrows and how long it stays productive. These four steps take about 10 minutes and make a real difference over a season.

01

Use clean, sharp scissors

Dull scissors crush the stem rather than cutting it cleanly — a crushed cut heals more slowly and creates an entry point for disease. Sharp scissors or pruning shears make a clean cut that the plant seals within hours. Wipe blades with rubbing alcohol between plants if you're harvesting multiple herb varieties; this prevents the spread of any fungal issues.

02

Cut just above a leaf node

A leaf node is a point on the stem where a pair of leaves is attached. When you cut just above a leaf node, the plant produces two new stems from that point — branching rather than just regrowing the same single stem. This is how you build a bushier, more productive plant over time. Never just snap or pull mint stems; always cut.

03

Take no more than one-third at once

As a general rule, never remove more than one-third of the plant's total mass in a single harvest. Mint recovers quickly, but aggressive cutting without recovery time — especially during heat waves — can set the plant back. If you need a large amount, harvest in two sessions a week apart rather than stripping the plant all at once.

04

Pinch flower buds immediately

Check the tops of stems every time you're near the plant. Flower buds appear as tight clusters at the growing tip, distinct from the flatter, paired leaves. Pinch them off the moment they appear — don't wait until they open. A stem that has bolted is not a lost cause; cut it all the way back to the base and it will regrow from the roots with fresh, pre-flowering leaves.

Short-term storage

Keeping Freshly Cut Mint Fresh

For same-day and same-week use, the goal is to slow the degradation of essential oils and keep the leaves from wilting. These methods work for 5 to 7 days — long enough to cover a week of regular cocktail use.

Method 1

The flower vase method (best for 5–7 days)

Trim the stem ends at a slight angle. Stand the sprigs in a glass or jar with about an inch of water at the bottom — just enough to cover the stems, not the leaves. Cover the leaves loosely with a plastic bag and refrigerate. Change the water every 2 days. This is the most effective short-term storage method for cocktail garnish use and keeps leaves looking fresh and perky.

Method 2

Damp paper towel (up to 3 days)

Wrap loosely in a barely damp paper towel and seal in a zip-lock bag in the refrigerator. Simpler than the vase method and works well for 2 to 3 days. Good for larger quantities of leaves that you've already stripped from the stems. Don't make the towel too wet — soggy conditions accelerate decay.

Method 3

Room temperature (same day only)

For mint you're using the same day, stand the sprigs in a glass of water at room temperature and leave on the counter. Cold refrigeration actually slows the opening of the leaves slightly — leaving mint at room temp for a few hours before use lets the leaves relax fully, which makes them easier to slap as a garnish and slightly more aromatic.

The garnish rule

Cut your garnish mint the day you're serving drinks. Even mint that has been stored well for 5 days in water is noticeably less aromatic than a stem cut an hour before use. If the mint is going on top of a drink where guests will smell it with every sip, cut it fresh on the day. Use stored mint for muddling and syrup-making, where the exact aromatic quality matters less.

Long-term

Preserving the Harvest

When the plant produces more than you can use fresh — and it will — these preservation methods let you extend the season into winter. Each one has a different use case in the bar.

🫙
Mint Simple Syrup
The most versatile preservation method. Equal parts sugar and water, steeped with fresh mint for 15 minutes, strained and refrigerated. Keeps 3 weeks in the fridge, 6 months in the freezer.
→ Use in mojitos, limeade, iced tea, juleps. Full recipe: Mint Simple Syrup
🧊
Mint Ice Cubes
Two approaches: freeze syrup in ice cube trays (each cube ≈ 1 oz, ready to use directly in a shaker) or freeze whole leaves in water for decorative herb ice cubes. The syrup cubes are more useful; the leaf cubes are more beautiful.
Freeze syrup cubes solid, then transfer to a bag. Label with date and type.
🫗
Mint-Infused Spirit
Pack a jar loosely with fresh mint leaves and cover with vodka or a neutral grain spirit. Steep at room temperature for 24 to 48 hours — taste frequently and strain when flavour is where you want it. Keeps indefinitely.
Use ½ oz in place of mint syrup in spirit-forward cocktails where you want the herb character without sweetness.
What doesn't work

Drying is the least effective preservation method for cocktail mint. The essential oils that give mint its brightness — the compounds that make a mojito taste alive — are volatile and largely destroyed by the heat of drying. Dried mint in a syrup produces a flat, tea-like flavour. It works for cooking but not for drinks. If you have surplus mint, choose the freezer over the dehydrator every time.

Common problems

Harvest Mistakes to Avoid

Most mint problems at the bar trace back to something that happened in the garden. Here are the ones that come up most often.

Mistake 01
Letting it flower before you harvest
Once mint flowers, the leaves turn bitter and the plant slows leaf production. Most home gardeners only notice once they're muddling leaves that taste medicinal and harsh.
Fix: Harvest every 2 to 3 weeks regardless of whether you need the mint. Give surplus to neighbours or make syrup. Regular cutting is the only reliable way to prevent bolting.
Mistake 02
Harvesting in the heat of the day
Afternoon harvests produce noticeably less aromatic mint. The heat causes partial evaporation of essential oils — the same compounds that make the drink smell and taste alive — before you've even cut the stem.
Fix: Harvest in the morning. Even moving your cutting time from 3pm to 9am produces a measurable difference in the aroma of fresh garnish mint.
Mistake 03
Storing cut mint in a sealed bag without water
Sealing mint in a dry plastic bag in the refrigerator causes the leaves to wilt quickly, turning them limp and dark. A closed environment without moisture accelerates decay.
Fix: Always store cut mint with either water in a jar (flower vase method) or wrapped in a barely damp paper towel. Dry storage and mint don't work together.
Mistake 04
Over-muddling
Pressing the muddler too hard and crushing the stems releases chlorophyll and bitter plant compounds that overpower the mint's clean flavour. This is the most common reason a mojito tastes harsh.
Fix: Muddle gently — you're pressing the oils out of the leaves, not crushing the plant. Or skip muddling entirely and use mint simple syrup instead for a consistently clean result.
Mistake 05
Trying to dry mint for cocktails
Dried mint loses most of the volatile aromatic compounds that make it useful in drinks. It's fine for cooking and tea but produces a flat, medicinal flavour in cocktails — nothing like fresh.
Fix: Freeze it instead. Make a syrup and freeze in ice cube trays. One cube per drink all winter, straight from the freezer into the shaker.
Mistake 06
Skipping the slap on garnish mint
Placing an unbruised mint sprig in a drink produces almost no aroma. Mint releases its essential oils when the cells are lightly broken — a garnish that isn't activated is mostly decoration.
Fix: Slap the sprig firmly against your palm just before placing it in the drink. You'll smell the difference immediately — and so will whoever you're serving.
