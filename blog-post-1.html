The Garden Bed Cocktail
Starter Guide

May 28, 2026  ·  6 min read

You don't need raised beds, a big yard, or any gardening experience to grow a great bar garden. What you need is five plants, a few containers, and a spot that gets six hours of sun. That's it. By mid-summer you'll have fresh ingredients within arm's reach that will make every drink you pour noticeably better.

This guide is for anyone starting from zero. We're going to cover exactly what to plant right now — late May — why each plant earns its spot, and how to keep them producing all season.

Start Here: The Five-Plant Bar Garden

If you only plant five things, plant these. They're the highest-return plants for cocktail use — easy to grow, hard to kill, and they show up in more recipes than anything else.

1. Mint

Mint is the most useful plant in the bar garden and the one most people plant wrong. It spreads aggressively through underground runners and will take over any bed it shares. Grow it in its own pot — a five-gallon container is perfect — and it becomes one of the most generous plants you'll ever tend. Cut it regularly and it grows back fuller. Ignore it and it grows back anyway.

Spearmint is the mildest and most versatile. Peppermint is stronger and better for syrups. Both work beautifully in mojitos, juleps, smashes, and any garnish that needs a pop of green and fragrance. See our full mint guide →

Planting tip

Plant mint in a 5-gallon pot with good drainage. Water when the top inch of soil is dry. Harvest the top 3–4 inches of each stem — the plant will branch out and produce twice as much.

2. Basil

Basil is the trickiest plant on this list — not because it's difficult, but because it has one hard rule: it hates cold. Temperatures below 50°F will stunt it, and a hard chill will kill it. In late May, the timing is right. Nights are warm enough and basil will take off.

Plant it in the sunniest spot you have. Six hours of direct sun is the minimum; eight is better. Give it good soil, consistent water, and pinch the flower buds the moment they appear. A single basil plant managed this way will produce more than most households can use.

Never refrigerate fresh basil — it blackens within hours. Store cut stems in a glass of water at room temperature, like flowers. It will last a week this way.

3. Rosemary

Rosemary is the most low-maintenance plant on this list. It wants full sun, poor-to-moderate soil, and excellent drainage. It does not want to sit in wet soil — that's the one thing that kills it. Get the drainage right and a rosemary plant can live for ten years, growing into a substantial shrub that produces year-round in mild climates.

In the bar, rosemary goes into syrups, infusions, and garnishes. Lightly slapping a sprig between your palms before garnishing a drink releases the oils and transforms the aromatic experience of the cocktail.

4. Lemon Thyme

Common thyme is great. Lemon thyme is better for cocktails. The lemon fragrance is bright, floral, and distinctly different from regular thyme — and it pairs beautifully with bourbon, gin, and honey. It's a perennial that comes back every year, tolerates drought, and thrives in a sunny container or garden edge.

Make a lemon thyme syrup in July when it's at peak flavor. Steep 8–10 sprigs in hot simple syrup for ten minutes, strain, and refrigerate. It keeps for three weeks and makes a Bourbon Smash taste like something a professional made.

5. Lavender

Lavender is the most rewarding plant in the bar garden when you get the conditions right, and the most frustrating when you don't. The condition it needs above all else is drainage. Lavender grows naturally in rocky, sandy Mediterranean soils. It does not want to sit in moisture.

Plant it in a raised bed or container with added perlite or grit mixed into the soil. Give it full sun and leave plenty of space — it needs air circulation. In return, it will bloom reliably every May and June, producing more than you can use fresh. Dry the surplus on screens or hung upside down in a warm spot, and you'll have lavender for syrups year-round.

Harvest timing

Cut lavender stems when the buds are just opening — not fully bloomed. The fragrance oils are most concentrated at this stage. Once flowers fully open and begin to drop, the peak has passed.

What to Do Right Now

It's late May. Here's the action list:

  • Buy mint and plant it immediately in its own container
  • Buy basil starts (not seeds — no time to start from seed now) and plant in your sunniest spot
  • Buy a rosemary plant and put it somewhere dry and sunny — it will largely take care of itself
  • Find lemon thyme at a good nursery and plant it alongside rosemary
  • Plant lavender now while it's still in bloom — you can harvest some this year

By July, every one of these plants will be producing more than you can use fresh. That's when you make syrups, infuse spirits, and freeze what you can't consume. The garden does the work. You just have to show up and harvest.
