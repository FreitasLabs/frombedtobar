Cocktail Herb Guide

How to Grow Basil for
Cocktails & Mocktails

Basil is the summer herb that doesn't hold back. Sweet, peppery, and faintly anise-like — it muddles beautifully, makes one of the great cocktail syrups, and has a natural affinity with strawberry that turns two simple ingredients into something you'll want to drink all season long. Grow it in a warm spot, pick it often, and never let it flower.

Why Grow Basil?

Every herb in the bar garden has its season. Basil's is summer — big, bold, and brief. It's the herb of long evenings, of strawberries and gin, of drinks that taste like the warmest part of the year. For the full picture on cocktail herbs, see the Cocktail Herb Guide.

🍓
The strawberry partnership
Basil and strawberry is one of the great garden pairings — the sweet, peppery herb amplifies the fruit's fragrance and makes both taste more of themselves. A Strawberry Basil Smash or a strawberry basil lemonade are not novelties; they are genuinely well-calibrated combinations that belong on every summer bar.
🧉
The best muddling herb
Basil's large, soft leaves release their oils with just a few gentle presses — no technique required, no risk of bitterness from woody stems. It's the easiest herb to muddle well, and it rewards even a beginner with a clean, aromatic result that transforms a simple gin sour into something worth talking about.
🌏
Multiple varieties, multiple drinks
Sweet basil, Thai basil, and lemon basil are genuinely different at the bar. Sweet basil is clean and versatile. Thai basil brings spice and anise that pairs beautifully with tequila and rum. Lemon basil adds citrus freshness for gin drinks. Three small plants, three distinct flavour profiles — more range than almost any other cocktail herb.
⚡
Fast and prolific
Basil goes from seedling to first harvest in 3 to 4 weeks in warm conditions. A single plant, pinched correctly, will produce enough leaves for multiple cocktails every week throughout the summer. Few bar garden herbs deliver such a high volume of usable material from such a small growing space.
🌿
Exceptional in mocktails
Basil's aromatic complexity carries beautifully in alcohol-free drinks — it provides the interest that a spirit would otherwise supply. Basil lemonade, strawberry basil soda, and basil honey sparkling water are drinks that feel complete and sophisticated without any alcohol. Browse our full recipe collection.
🏺
Syrup that freezes beautifully
Basil doesn't dry well, but basil simple syrup captures the fresh flavour perfectly and freezes for up to three months. One productive afternoon at the end of summer — basil picked, syrup made, cubes frozen — means Basil Smashes in October from your own garden. That's worth planning for.

Choose your plant

Which Basil Variety is Best for Drinks?

Basil variety selection matters more than most people realise for cocktail use. The flavour differences between sweet, Thai, and lemon basil are significant enough to produce quite different drinks from the same spirit base. Grow more than one if space allows.

Genovese (Sweet Basil)
Most versatile
Flavour
Sweet, peppery, and slightly clove-like. The classic basil — clean, broad-spectrum, and works in virtually every cocktail application.
Best for
Muddling, syrups, Basil Smash, strawberry cocktails, gin drinks
Growth
Large leaves that are easy to muddle and visually striking as a garnish. The most productive and widely available variety.
Thai Basil
Best for spice
Flavour
Spicier, more anise-forward than Genovese. The assertive, almost liquorice-like character works in a completely different set of drinks.
Best for
Tequila and rum cocktails, Thai basil margaritas, spiced syrups, anything with heat or citrus
Growth
Purple stems, smaller and sturdier than Genovese. Slower to bolt in heat — often productive longer into the season.
Lemon Basil
Best for citrus drinks
Flavour
Light, citrus-herb — a clean lemon note layered over a gentle basil base. Less sweet and peppery than Genovese, more aromatic and floral.
Best for
Gin fizzes, lemon cocktails, sparkling mocktails, any drink where you want herb without weight
Growth
Smaller, lighter leaves than Genovese. Bolts slightly faster in heat — worth growing alongside Genovese for sequential harvest.
Purple / Dark Opal Basil
Garnish variety
Flavour
Similar to Genovese but slightly more clove-forward and a little less sweet. The deep purple-black colour is the main attraction for bar use.
Best for
Garnish — the striking colour against a clear or pale drink is visually exceptional. Purple basil in a gin and tonic or a vodka spritz is one of the most striking garden garnishes.
Note
Grow alongside Genovese — use purple for garnish, Genovese for muddling and syrup.

In the ground

How to Grow Basil

Basil is warm-weather growing distilled into a single plant. Get it warm, keep it picked, and it will produce generously all summer. Let it get cold, crowded, or flowery, and it fails fast.

What basil needs

  • Heat: The most important factor. Basil needs soil and air temperatures consistently above 65°F to grow well. Below this it sulks; below 50°F it blackens. Plant outside only after your last frost date.
  • Sun: Full sun — 6 to 8 hours minimum. In partial shade, basil grows slowly and with less fragrance.
  • Water: Consistent moisture without waterlogging. Water at the base, not from above — wet leaves in cool conditions invite downy mildew. In containers, check daily in hot weather.
  • Soil: Rich and well-draining. Basil, unlike Mediterranean herbs, benefits from a fertile soil — add compost at planting and feed lightly through the season.
  • Spacing: 10–12 inches apart in the ground. In containers, one plant per 8-inch pot for best results — supermarket plants packed with multiple seedlings need separating.
  • Pinching: Begin pinching flower buds the moment they appear, and continue throughout the season. This is the single most important task for a productive basil plant.

Common growing problems

  • Cold damage and blackening
    Even a single cold night below 50°F can blacken basil leaves. Plants bought from supermarkets are often already stressed from cold storage — bring them home, pot them up, and keep them on a warm windowsill for a week before putting outside.
  • Allowing the plant to flower
    A flowering basil plant is a plant shifting out of leaf production. Leaves become smaller, tougher, and more bitter. Pinch flowers immediately and consistently — every few days during peak summer. One week of neglect can send the plant fully to flower.
  • Overwatering in cool weather
    In warm weather, basil uses water quickly and needs regular watering. In cool or cloudy spells, the same watering schedule creates waterlogged soil that rots the roots. Adjust watering to the weather.
  • Crowded supermarket pots
    Supermarket basil pots typically contain 10 to 20 seedlings crammed together. Grown as-is, they compete for nutrients and light and quickly deteriorate. Separate and repot into individual containers — each seedling becomes a full productive plant.
The supermarket basil trick

One pot of supermarket basil, properly separated and repotted, becomes 8 to 12 individual plants — enough to supply a whole summer of cocktails. Let the seedlings recover on a warm, bright windowsill for a week before potting up. Feed lightly once established, pinch regularly, and you'll have far more basil than you expect from a $3–4 nursery pot.

From the plant

When and How to Harvest

Basil rewards frequent, decisive harvesting. The more you pick, the more it produces. The less you pick, the sooner it flowers and ends the season.

Step 01

Always harvest from the top

Cut or pinch the topmost growing tip of each stem — the point where the stem forks and new leaves are emerging. Cutting here causes the plant to branch from just below the cut, producing two new stems where there was one. This is how a basil plant becomes bushy and productive rather than tall and leggy.

Step 02

Cut above a pair of leaves

When harvesting, always cut just above a node — the point where two leaves meet the stem. The plant will branch from this point. If you cut below the node into bare stem, that section won't regenerate. Take 4 to 6 leaves per cut, leaving at least two pairs of leaves below to sustain the plant's growth.

Step 03

Harvest in the morning

Essential oils are most concentrated in the morning before the heat of the day begins to volatilise them. Morning basil is measurably more aromatic than basil cut in the afternoon. This is worth observing particularly when making syrup or harvesting for same-day muddling.

Step 04

Pinch flowers immediately and completely

The moment you see any flower spike forming — even a tiny bud cluster at the top of a stem — pinch the entire flower head off. Don't leave partial flower stems; remove the whole spike back to just above a pair of leaves. Check every 3 to 4 days during peak summer. One missed week is often enough to send the whole plant to flower.

Step 05

Make a big end-of-season harvest

When temperatures begin to cool in late summer and the plant starts to slow, make one large harvest before the first cold night takes the plant. Strip most of the remaining leaves and make a large batch of basil syrup — freeze it in ice cube trays. This final harvest extends your basil supply weeks or months past the growing season.

Step 06

Use fresh — basil wilts within hours

Harvested basil leaves wilt quickly at room temperature. For garnishes, cut stems and store them in a glass of water at room temperature — not in the fridge, which blackens the leaves. Use muddling leaves the same day they're picked. For syrup, make it immediately after harvest for the best colour and flavour.

Make it last

Preserving Your Basil Harvest

Basil doesn't dry well — but it freezes beautifully as a syrup. A productive summer harvest handled right will keep the Basil Smash alive well into fall.

🫙
Basil Simple Syrup
Equal parts sugar and water, brought to a simmer until dissolved. Remove from heat, add a large handful of fresh basil leaves (20 to 30), and steep for 15 to 20 minutes. The syrup will turn a beautiful pale green. Strain, cool, and refrigerate — or freeze immediately in ice cube trays for longer storage.
Keeps 10 to 14 days refrigerated. Freeze for up to 3 months. The flavour is best in the first week.
🧊
Syrup Ice Cubes
The best way to extend basil's season beyond summer. Pour finished basil syrup into ice cube trays and freeze. Each cube is roughly 1 oz. Drop directly into the shaker with your other ingredients — it dilutes exactly as it should as the drink shakes. October Basil Smashes are genuinely possible with a summer cube stash.
Label trays the day you freeze them. Basil syrup cubes and mint syrup cubes look identical and produce very different cocktails.
🍓
Strawberry Basil Shrub
Combine 250g hulled strawberries, 1 cup sugar, and a large handful of basil leaves. Muddle together and refrigerate for 24 to 48 hours. Strain and add 120ml apple cider vinegar. The result is a drinking vinegar — sharp, fruity, and herbal — that transforms sparkling water into one of the best non-alcoholic summer drinks you can make from the garden.
Keeps refrigerated for up to 6 weeks. Use 1 to 2 tablespoons in sparkling water, or add to gin or vodka for a more complex cocktail.
🫗
Basil-Infused Vodka
Pack a clean jar with fresh basil leaves and cover with vodka. Infuse for 4 to 6 hours at room temperature, tasting regularly. Basil infuses quickly — longer than this and it can turn bitter and grassy. Strain, bottle, and use within 2 months. Excellent in a Basil Martini or anywhere a clean herbal note is welcome.
Use a neutral, clean vodka so the basil can lead. Don't use gin — the botanicals compete rather than complement.
🌿
Basil Oil (Garnish)
Blend a large handful of basil leaves with 100ml of a neutral oil until smooth, then strain through a fine cloth or coffee filter. The resulting bright green oil keeps for a week in the fridge and works as a dramatic cocktail garnish — a few drops floated on the surface of a clear drink, or swirled on a white foam, is visually striking and subtly fragrant.
Blanch the basil for 30 seconds in boiling water and immediately plunge into ice water before blending — this sets the green colour so it doesn't brown.
🍯
Basil Honey
Gently warm honey (don't boil) and steep 8 to 10 fresh basil leaves for 20 to 30 minutes. The basil note is subtle in honey — more of a background whisper than the clear hit you get in a syrup. Works well in drinks where honey is already the sweetener and you want a gentle herbal lift without adding a separate syrup.
Basil honey and lemon in sparkling water is a quietly excellent mocktail that takes five minutes to make and tastes like considerably more thought went into it.

At the bar

Using Basil in Drinks

Basil is the most approachable herb to use at the bar. It muddled cleanly, syrups simply, and garnishes beautifully. Here's how to get the most from a summer's worth of leaves.

🧉
Muddling
The most direct way to use basil at the bar. Place 6 to 8 large leaves in the bottom of a shaker, press firmly with a muddler 4 to 5 times (enough to bruise, not pulverise), add ice and your other ingredients, shake hard, and double-strain through a fine mesh strainer. The result is cleaner and brighter than any syrup.
Don't muddle stems — leaves only. And always double-strain; basil fragments in a finished drink are unpleasant and avoidable.
🫙
Simple Syrup
Basil syrup is the most versatile preparation — it can go in any drink where fresh muddling isn't practical, and it allows for precise control of the basil intensity. Start with half an ounce and increase. It's particularly useful for building drinks in batches or for making a non-alcoholic version of a cocktail at the same time as the alcoholic one.
Make syrup in small batches so it's always fresh. Two weeks old basil syrup is noticeably less vibrant than week-one syrup.
🌿
Leaf Garnish
A single large Genovese basil leaf or a sprig of purple basil is one of the most beautiful cocktail garnishes from the summer bar garden. Slap the leaf lightly against your palm before placing it — this bruises the cells and releases fragrance. The nose experiences the basil before the palate touches the drink.
For maximum visual impact, float a large whole leaf on the surface of a drink in a coupe glass rather than placing it over the rim.
🍓
Fruit Pairing
Basil's greatest partnership is with strawberry — the herb's peppery sweetness amplifies the fruit's fragrance in a way that makes both taste more intensely of themselves. Muddle them together, combine as syrup and juice, or build a shrub. Peach and basil is a close second. Watermelon and basil is exceptional in a gin or tequila cocktail in high summer.
When muddling basil with fruit, add the basil first, muddle gently, then add the fruit and muddle together. This layers the flavours rather than blending them into one undifferentiated note.
🍸
The Basil Smash Method
The Basil Smash is built around maximum basil extraction: a large handful of leaves muddled hard in the shaker before adding gin, lemon, and syrup. The result is a very green, very aromatic drink. The technique can be applied to any sour-format cocktail — swap the gin for vodka, tequila, or make it alcohol-free with elderflower and lemon.
Use 10 to 12 large Genovese leaves for a full Smash — more than you think you need. The flavour mellows after shaking and straining.
🫧
Basil Soda
Basil syrup, fresh lemon juice, and sparkling water is the simplest and most refreshing alcohol-free basil drink from the garden. It takes less than two minutes to assemble and produces something that tastes like a thoughtfully designed beverage rather than a hastily assembled mocktail. Add a slapped basil leaf as garnish and serve in a tall glass over plenty of ice.
A pinch of flaky salt in the glass before building amplifies the basil's sweetness and adds a savory depth that makes the drink feel more complete.

In the glass

What to Make with Garden Basil

Basil's best drinks are built on simplicity — the herb is vivid enough that it doesn't need many companions. Start with the Basil Smash and build from there.

Basil Smash
Cocktail · Modern Classic
Gin, fresh lemon juice, simple syrup, and a generous handful of fresh basil — shaken hard and double-strained. One of the most celebrated garden-to-glass cocktails in modern bartending. The basil note is unmistakable and the lemon keeps everything bright and alive. Make it with Genovese basil for the clearest, sweetest result.
Spirit: London Dry Gin  ·  Basil: 10–12 large Genovese leaves
Strawberry Basil Margarita
Cocktail · Summer
Tequila blanco, fresh lime juice, strawberry basil syrup, and a salted rim. The strawberry-basil combination works even better in a margarita than in a gin drink — the tequila's herbal agave character aligns perfectly with both the fruit and the herb. Make the syrup by steeping basil and macerated strawberries together, then straining before combining with simple syrup.
Spirit: Tequila Blanco  ·  Rim: Salt and a touch of black pepper
Basil Gimlet
Cocktail · Clean and Sharp
Gin, fresh lime juice, and basil syrup — shaken hard, double-strained into a chilled coupe, and garnished with a whole basil leaf floated on the surface. Where the Basil Smash is vivid and green, the Basil Gimlet is elegant and precise. The lime's sharpness cuts beautifully through basil's sweetness.
Spirit: London Dry Gin  ·  Garnish: One large whole basil leaf floated
Thai Basil Paloma
Cocktail · Tequila
Tequila blanco, fresh grapefruit juice, Thai basil syrup, a squeeze of lime, and grapefruit soda over ice. Thai basil's anise and spice character cuts through the grapefruit bitterness in a way that sweet basil can't quite manage. Garnish with a Thai basil sprig — the purple stems are particularly striking against a pale pink drink.
Spirit: Tequila Blanco  ·  Basil: Thai basil specifically
Strawberry Basil Lemonade
Mocktail · Summer Favourite
Fresh lemon juice, strawberry basil syrup, and sparkling water — one of the best drinks from the garden, full stop. The combination is vivid, fragrant, and refreshing in a way that satisfies on a hot day without any alcohol needed. Use fresh strawberries blended into the syrup rather than strawberry juice for the most intensely flavoured result.
Add a splash of elderflower cordial to round out the flavour and add floral complexity
Basil Vodka Soda
Cocktail · Simple Build
Basil-infused vodka or plain vodka with basil syrup, fresh lemon, and sparkling water over ice — a long, light drink that lets basil lead without complication. Built in the glass rather than shaken, so the basil stays fragrant and the bubbles stay present. A slapped basil sprig as garnish and nothing more is needed.
Spirit: Basil-infused vodka or plain vodka with 0.5 oz basil syrup

Learn from these

Common Basil Mistakes

Basil is generous when you understand it and difficult when you don't. These are the mistakes most worth avoiding.

Mistake 01
Planting out too early
The most common cause of early basil failure. Cold nights stunt or kill the plant before it's established. Many gardeners plant in early May when daytime temperatures are warm but nights still dip below 50°F.
Fix: Wait until night temperatures are consistently above 50°F before planting outside. When in doubt, wait another week — a late-planted basil in warm soil overtakes an early-planted stressed one within two weeks.
Mistake 02
Letting it flower
Once basil flowers, leaf production slows and the flavour of the remaining leaves becomes more bitter and complex in a way that doesn't work as well at the bar. A week of inattention during peak summer can send the whole plant to flower.
Fix: Check every 3 to 4 days during July and August and pinch any flower buds immediately and completely. This is the most important single task for a productive basil season.
Mistake 03
Refrigerating fresh basil
Basil is cold-sensitive — the fridge blackens and wilts it within hours. Many people store cut basil in the fridge like other salad herbs and are confused when it deteriorates so quickly.
Fix: Store cut basil stems in a glass of water at room temperature, out of direct sun, like a bunch of flowers. It will keep for 2 to 3 days in good condition this way.
Mistake 04
Not double-straining after muddling
Basil leaves break up during muddling and shaking, releasing small green fragments. A single hawthorne strainer doesn't catch them all — the resulting drink has visible green specks and a slightly fibrous texture that detracts from the drinking experience.
Fix: Always double-strain muddled basil cocktails through a hawthorne strainer AND a fine mesh tea strainer simultaneously. The result is a clear, clean drink with full basil flavour.
Mistake 05
Growing only one variety
Many gardeners grow only Genovese basil and never experience what Thai basil does in a tequila drink or what lemon basil contributes to a gin fizz. These are significantly different flavour profiles, not subtle variations on the same theme.
Fix: Grow at least two varieties side by side. The care requirements are identical and the range of drinks you can make doubles immediately.
Mistake 06
Making no end-of-season harvest
Many basil plants are lost to the first cold night of fall with most of their remaining leaves unused. A late-season harvest and a batch of frozen syrup cubes could have extended the basil season by months.
Fix: Watch the forecast in late summer. When a cool night is coming, strip the plant and make syrup. Freeze the cubes. Your October self will be grateful.

Questions answered

Basil Growing FAQ

The questions that come up most often — answered plainly.

Basil is easy to grow in the right conditions — warmth, sun, and consistent moisture. The catch is that it's more demanding about temperature than most bar garden herbs. It sulks in cool weather, blackens in cold, and fails to thrive in anything less than a warm, sheltered spot. Get the heat right and it grows prolifically. Ignore it and it flowers early, becomes bitter, and dies with the first cool night of fall.

Genovese (sweet basil) is the most versatile for cocktail use — the large, glossy leaves are easy to muddle, the flavour is clean and sweet, and it works across gin, vodka, and tequila drinks. Thai basil brings a spicier, more anise-forward character that pairs especially well with tequila and rum, and works in a different set of drinks entirely. Lemon basil adds a light citrus note that makes it excellent in gin fizzes and lemonade cocktails. If you have space, grow all three.

It's gin, fresh lemon juice, simple syrup, and a generous handful of fresh basil leaves — shaken hard with ice and double-strained. The basil is both muddled in the shaker and used as a garnish. It's one of the most celebrated garden-to-glass cocktails in modern bartending and the best single showcase for what fresh basil does at the bar.

For a guaranteed supply, start fresh seeds each year or buy seedlings in late spring.
