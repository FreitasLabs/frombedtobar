Hibiscus Paloma

Tequila blanco, fresh grapefruit, hibiscus-infused syrup, and a honey-salt rim. The drink that turns deep ruby in the glass and tastes like a sun-drenched afternoon. Grow your own hibiscus or find dried flowers at any Latin grocery store.

🌺 Hibiscus 🍊 Grapefruit Tequila Blanco Summer 10 min

The Recipe

Hibiscus Paloma

This is a shaken-and-built drink — the base gets shaken, then topped with grapefruit soda in the glass. The honey-salt rim is not optional. It's what ties the tartness of the hibiscus and grapefruit together and makes each sip complete.

  • 2 oz tequila blanco (or mezcal for a smoky variation)
  • 🌺 1 oz hibiscus syrup (recipe below)
  • 1 oz fresh grapefruit juice
  • ½ oz fresh lime juice
  • 2 oz grapefruit soda
  • Rim: 1 tbsp honey + ½ tsp flaky salt
  • Garnish: grapefruit wedge
  • 🌺 1½ oz hibiscus syrup
  • 1 oz fresh grapefruit juice
  • ½ oz fresh lime juice
  • 1 oz strong chilled hibiscus tea
  • 3 oz grapefruit soda
  • Rim: 1 tbsp honey + ½ tsp flaky salt
  • Garnish: grapefruit wedge
First, rim the glass: spread a thin layer of honey on a small flat plate and flaky salt on another. Run a grapefruit wedge around the rim of a tall glass, dip in honey, then lightly in salt. Fill the glass with ice. Next, combine tequila (or hibiscus tea for mocktail), hibiscus syrup, grapefruit juice, and lime juice in a shaker with ice. Shake for 10 seconds. Strain into the prepared glass over the ice. Top slowly with grapefruit soda. Stir once gently with a bar spoon from the bottom. Garnish with a grapefruit wedge and serve immediately while the drink is still ruby-bright.

The Foundation

Hibiscus Simple Syrup

This syrup is what gives the paloma its deep ruby color and its tart, cranberry-like backbone. It's also useful in countless other applications — lemonade, sparkling water, and gin cocktails all benefit from a splash. Make a double batch and keep it in the fridge.

Syrup Recipe
Hibiscus Simple Syrup
  • 1 cup water
  • 1 cup white sugar
  • 🌺 ½ cup dried hibiscus flowers (jamaica)
Combine water and sugar in a small saucepan over medium heat. Stir until sugar fully dissolves. Add dried hibiscus flowers, remove from heat immediately, and steep for 20 minutes — the syrup will turn a beautiful deep crimson. The longer you steep, the more tart and intensely flavored it becomes. Strain through a fine mesh sieve, pressing gently on the flowers. Cool completely, bottle, and refrigerate. The color fades from the brightest ruby within a few days in the fridge — this is normal and the flavor is unaffected.
Keeps refrigerated 2–3 weeks · Makes about 1.5 cups · Also excellent in lemonade, gin drinks, and sparkling water

In the Garden

Growing Hibiscus

Hibiscus sabdariffa — the roselle species used for cocktails — is a stunning annual that's grown more easily than most people expect. The dried flowers (jamaica) are the ingredient; the fresh calyces from a home harvest have a more complex, deeper flavor than anything you'll buy commercially.

🌺
Growing Roselle
Hibiscus sabdariffa (roselle) is a warm-season annual. Plant after the last frost in a full-sun location with well-draining soil. It grows tall — up to 6 feet in a good season — and produces beautiful ivory flowers followed by the fleshy red calyces that are the cocktail ingredient. Water consistently and fertilize lightly through the growing season.
✂️
Harvesting Calyces
Harvest the calyces — the fleshy red structures surrounding the base of each flower — when they're plump, deeply red, and firm. The flower itself will have already dropped; you're harvesting the calyx that remains. Twist or cut them off cleanly. Fresh calyces can be used immediately for syrups; for year-round use, dry them completely in a dehydrator at 95°F until crisp.
🌞
Finding Jamaica
If you're not growing your own, dried hibiscus flowers (jamaica) are widely available at Latin grocery stores for a fraction of what specialty stores charge. Look for deep ruby-red flowers — faded brownish hibiscus makes a weak syrup. A pound of dried hibiscus goes a long way: you'll make this syrup many times over before running out.

Bartender Notes

Tips for getting it right

The Rim
Honey before salt — not the other way around
The honey acts as the adhesive for the salt. Dip the rim in honey first (you don't need much — just a thin coating), then in flaky salt. Table salt is too fine and will make the rim taste overwhelmingly salty. Flaky sea salt (Maldon is ideal) gives you discrete salt crystals that hit your palate in bursts rather than all at once.
Color
Pour tonic slowly to preserve the color
The beautiful gradient in this drink — deep ruby hibiscus at the bottom lightening to pink toward the grapefruit soda on top — depends on how you pour the soda. Add it slowly down the side of the glass without stirring immediately. Let it layer for a moment before one gentle stir from the bottom. The color photograph is better than the fully stirred version, and it tastes the same.
Grapefruit
Fresh squeezed, not from a carton
The difference between fresh-squeezed grapefruit juice and the carton version is especially pronounced here because the hibiscus is already assertive and tart. Fresh grapefruit has a brightness and a slight bitterness that carton juice doesn't replicate. Squeeze it the day you make the drink. Red grapefruit is sweeter; white grapefruit is more bitter — either works, adjust the syrup amount slightly if using a very tart white grapefruit.
Variation
Try mezcal instead of tequila
The smoky, slightly earthy character of mezcal and the tart, floral depth of hibiscus have a natural affinity. A mezcal hibiscus paloma tastes like a more complex, grown-up version of the tequila original — the smoke adds a savory dimension that makes the grapefruit and hibiscus flavors read differently. Use a mild, approachable mezcal rather than a heavily smoked expression.

Serve It With

Food & Occasion Pairings

🌮
Food
Fish tacos with mango salsa. Ceviche. Elote corn. Guacamole. Anything with bright citrus or a little heat — the hibiscus is tart and floral enough to cut through richness and hold its own against spice.
🏖️
Occasion
Weekend afternoons, outdoor gatherings, anything al fresco in summer. This is the drink that looks as good as it tastes — the ruby color in a clear glass makes it the most photogenic thing on the table. Perfect for a crowd because the syrup batches easily.
🌅
Best Time
The golden hour of a summer afternoon, when the light hits a glass of deep ruby liquid just right. Also: the drink to make when someone says they don't like tequila, because the hibiscus and grapefruit tend to change their mind fairly quickly.
