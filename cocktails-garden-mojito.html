Garden Mojito

Rum, homegrown mint syrup, fresh lime, and soda. No muddling — just clean, bright flavour from the mint you grew yourself. This is what the garden tastes like in a glass.

The recipe

Garden Mojito

The classic mojito involves muddling fresh mint, adding simple syrup separately, and hoping the ratio works out. This version skips the muddle and uses homegrown mint simple syrup instead. The result is more consistent, less fuss, and tastes just as fresh — because the mint went from the garden into the syrup two days ago, not a warehouse three weeks ago. For everything you need to know about growing the mint, see the Mint Guide.

Step 01

Fill your glass with ice

Pack a highball glass with ice — cubes or cracked ice both work. A well-chilled glass keeps the drink cold longer and maintains the carbonation of the soda water. If you have a tall Collins glass, that's ideal.

Step 02

Add rum, mint syrup, and lime

Pour 2 oz of white rum over the ice. Add ¾ oz of homegrown mint simple syrup and ¾ oz of fresh-squeezed lime juice. Stir briefly — about 10 seconds — to combine and start chilling the mixture.

Fresh lime only. Bottled lime juice tastes flat and cooked next to fresh mint. This drink doesn't have enough ingredients to hide it.

Step 03

Top with soda water

Add 2 oz of chilled soda water — pour it gently down the inside of the glass rather than directly over the ice. This preserves the bubbles. Give one gentle stir from the bottom to integrate.

Step 04

Garnish and serve

Take a fresh sprig of mint from the garden. Hold it in one hand and slap it firmly against your other palm — this bruises the leaves slightly and releases the aromatic oils without breaking them down. Tuck it into the top of the drink so the leaves sit just above the rim. Add a lime wheel on the side.

The garnish mint isn't just decorative — your nose is over it with every sip. Fresh garden mint here is the difference between a good drink and a great one.

Why syrup instead of muddling?

Muddling is a technique, and it's easy to do it wrong. Over-muddle and you break the stems, releasing bitter chlorophyll. Under-muddle and you barely extract any flavour. Mint syrup gives you consistent, calibrated mint flavour every time — and you can taste and adjust before the drink is built. It also lets you prep ahead, which makes a difference when you're making a round for more than one person.

What You Need

  • White rum 2 oz
  • Mint simple syrup ¾ oz
  • Fresh lime juice ¾ oz
  • Soda water 2 oz
  • Fresh mint sprig Garnish — from the garden 1
  • Lime wheel Garnish 1
Glass & Ice Highball or Collins glass packed with ice. Serve immediately.
Mocktail? Skip the rum. Add 4 oz of soda water and a splash of coconut water. See the Mint Limeade for a dedicated alcohol-free version.

Spirit selection

Which Rum to Use

White rum is the classic choice — light enough to let the mint and lime lead. Here's how different bottles behave in this drink.

Best overall
Flor de Caña 4 Year
Clean, slightly sweet, and dry enough to let the mint come through. A solid everyday choice that doesn't compete with the garden ingredients.
Widely available
Bacardí Superior
The classic mojito rum — neutral, consistent, and designed for exactly this drink. Nothing wrong with using the most tested bottle in the world for a mojito.
More character
Plantation 3 Stars
A blend of three Caribbean rums — more flavour and complexity than a standard white rum. Works beautifully with fresh mint and makes the drink feel a bit more intentional.
Split base
1 oz white + 1 oz aged rum
Split the base between a white rum and a lightly aged rum like Appleton Signature. Adds a little vanilla and wood without overpowering the mint. A good option for cooler evenings.

Riff on it

Variations

The base recipe is a clean canvas. Each of these builds on it without changing the method — just swap one ingredient or add one more.

Garden Mojito Royale
Top with dry prosecco instead of soda water. More festive, less casual — good for a crowd or a celebratory occasion. Use slightly less mint syrup to account for the prosecco's own sweetness.
Cucumber Mint Mojito
Use cucumber mint syrup (see the syrup variations) instead of plain mint syrup. Add two thin cucumber slices to the glass with the ice. Cooling and very summery.
Spicy Mint Mojito
Use spicy mint syrup — made by adding jalapeño slices to the steep. The heat lingers behind the mint and lime and works unexpectedly well with rum. Pull the pepper early if you want less heat.

Alcohol-free

The Mocktail Version

The mint and lime do most of the flavour work in a mojito — the rum is structure, not the star. This makes the mocktail version genuinely good rather than an afterthought.

Virgin Garden Mojito

Replace the rum with a combination of 1 oz of fresh coconut water and 1 oz of white grape juice or apple juice — this gives you body, a hint of sweetness, and a little complexity without alcohol. Keep the mint syrup, lime juice, and soda water at the same ratios. Build the same way.

Alternatively, for the full dedicated mocktail experience with extra care given to flavour balance, see the Sparkling Mint Limeade — built from the ground up as an alcohol-free drink.
