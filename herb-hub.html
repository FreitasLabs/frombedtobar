The case for growing your own

Why Grow Herbs for Drinks?

Fresh herbs from the garden don't just taste better — they change what's possible at the bar. A store-bought mint sprig is fine. A handful of spearmint cut ten minutes ago is something else entirely. Here's why growing your own is worth it, even if you only have a few pots. See our garden tips page for practical growing advice.

🌿
Dramatically better flavour
Essential oils in herbs — the compounds that make them taste and smell the way they do — start to degrade the moment they're cut. Garden-fresh herbs have oils that shop-bought produce simply can't match. The difference in a mojito or a spritz is immediate.
💰
Lower cost per drink
A packet of supermarket herbs costs $3–5 and lasts a few days. A pot of mint costs the same and produces enough to make syrups all season. Rosemary and thyme are perennials — plant them once and harvest for years.
📅
Seasonal inspiration built in
When you grow your own, the garden tells you what to make. Lavender flowering in June means lavender syrups. Basil going gangbusters in August means basil gimlets. The season becomes your cocktail menu.
🪴
Easier than most vegetables
Herbs are among the most forgiving things you can grow. Most thrive on neglect, bounce back from bad haircuts, and don't need rich soil or perfect conditions. They're an ideal starting point for anyone new to gardening. Visit our planting guide for zone-by-zone timing.
🏠
Perfect for small spaces
You don't need a garden. A few pots on a sunny balcony or a bright windowsill will keep a home bar stocked all season. Mint, basil, thyme, and lemon balm all grow happily in containers. Six pots is more than enough.
🍹
Mocktails benefit just as much
Fresh herbs are arguably even more important in non-alcoholic drinks, where they carry more of the flavour load without a spirit to lean on. Lemon balm, mint, and basil make genuinely exceptional alcohol-free drinks when garden-fresh. Browse our full recipe collection.

Start here

Best Herbs for Beginners

If you're starting from nothing, these four herbs will give you the best return for the least effort. They're hard to kill, grow fast, and show up in more drink recipes than anything else you'll plant. A few pots in a sunny spot is all you need — see our garden tips for container growing advice.

01
Mint
Mint is nearly indestructible. It grows fast, comes back every year, and you can cut it hard without killing it. The one rule: always grow it in a container — it spreads aggressively underground and will take over a garden bed. A single pot of spearmint will supply more than enough for mojitos, juleps, syrups, and garnishes all season. It's also the most useful herb in non-alcoholic drinks.
02
Basil
Basil is the easiest annual cocktail herb. It loves warmth, grows quickly from a nursery seedling, and rewards regular harvesting. The only care it needs is pinching off flower buds as soon as they appear — this keeps the plant productive for months. Sweet basil works in more drink styles than people expect, and it's exceptional muddled with gin or vodka.
03
Rosemary
Rosemary is a perennial that thrives on neglect. Plant it once and it will come back every year in most climates, getting bigger and more productive each season. It's drought-tolerant, rarely gets pests, and doesn't need rich soil. Its flavour is bold enough to stand up to aged spirits like bourbon and rye — making it one of the most useful herbs for serious cocktail making.
04
Lemon Balm
Lemon balm is often overlooked, but it's one of the easiest herbs you can grow and one of the most useful for drinks. It spreads readily (like mint, container growing is sensible), tolerates partial shade better than most herbs, and produces gently lemony leaves that make exceptional mocktails and light spritz drinks. It's particularly good for anyone who wants alcohol-free options that don't feel like an afterthought.

Drink what's in season

Seasonal Herb Calendar

The garden tells you what to make. This is a drinking calendar — what each season offers and the kinds of drinks worth making with it. For planting and growing timing by zone, see the full planting guide.

🌸 Spring
Fresh starts and floral notes
Mint Lemon Balm Chives Tarragon
Spring is when mint and lemon balm wake up first — often before you're ready for them. Early harvests are the most tender and aromatic. Use them in light, bright drinks: spritzes, Collins-style cocktails, and sparkling mocktails. Lavender starts to bud late spring in warmer climates.
→ Make: Mint Smash · Lemon Balm Spritz · Herbal Sparkling Lemonade
☀️ Summer
Peak season — everything at once
Basil Lavender Mint Thyme Lemon Balm
Summer is the richest time for cocktail herbs. Basil peaks and goes quickly — make syrups and freeze them while you can. Lavender blooms in early summer and fades fast; harvest the flowers before they fully open for the best flavour. Thyme and mint are at their most productive. This is the time to make batches of syrup and infusions.
→ Make: Lavender Lemon Spritz · Basil Smash · Garden Herb Gimlet
🍂 Autumn
Robust herbs, warmer drinks
Rosemary Thyme Sage Mint
As temperatures drop, the annual herbs (basil, lemon balm) fade out and the perennials take over. Rosemary and thyme intensify in cooler weather and pair naturally with the warmer spirits — bourbon, rye, apple brandy — that feel right in autumn. Sage becomes an interesting cocktail herb in autumn, pairing well with brown spirits and apple.
→ Make: Rosemary Bourbon Sour · Sage Apple Toddy · Thyme Whiskey Smash
❄️ Winter
Work from your pantry
Rosemary Thyme Frozen Syrups Dried Lavender
In most climates, rosemary and thyme survive winter in the garden and can still be harvested lightly. Everything else comes from what you preserved in summer — frozen syrups, dried lavender, herb-infused spirits. This is the season for spirit-forward drinks where a single herbal element does a lot of work: a rosemary sprig in a Negroni, dried lavender in a hot toddy.
→ Make: Rosemary Negroni · Lavender Hot Toddy · Thyme Old Fashioned

From the garden to the glass

How to Harvest Herbs for Drinks

Harvesting herbs at the right time and in the right way makes a real difference to how they taste in a drink. These aren't complicated rules — just small habits that are worth building. For deeper growing and care advice, see Garden Tips.

01 — Timing
Harvest in the morning
Essential oils — the compounds that make herbs taste and smell the way they do — are most concentrated in the morning before the heat of the day causes them to evaporate. If you're making a syrup or batch cocktail for an evening, cut your herbs that morning and keep them in a glass of water like cut flowers until you need them.
02 — Technique
Cut, don't pull
Use clean scissors or snips to cut stems rather than pulling or tearing. For mint and basil, cut stems just above a leaf node — the plant will branch out from that point and become bushier. For rosemary and thyme, take individual stems from the tips. Never remove more than a third of a plant at once.
03 — Washing
Rinse gently, dry well
Rinse herbs under cool water and shake off excess moisture. For garnishes, pat dry with a paper towel — water droplets dilute the drink surface and look messy in a finished glass. For herbs going into a syrup, washing is fine and excess moisture doesn't matter since it will be cooked off.
04 — Storage
Keep them like flowers
Store cut herbs in a glass with an inch of water, loosely covered with a plastic bag, in the fridge. Basil is the exception — it's tropical and hates cold, so store it at room temperature on the counter. Most herbs kept this way will last 5 to 7 days, far longer than in a sealed bag.
05 — Preserving
Turn surplus into syrup
When you have more than you can use fresh, make simple syrups. Equal parts sugar and water, brought to a simmer, then steep the herbs off the heat for 15–30 minutes. Strain, bottle, refrigerate. Most herb syrups keep for 2–3 weeks. You can also freeze syrups in ice cube trays for longer storage — a great way to extend your summer harvest into winter.
06 — Drying
Dry what you can't use
Rosemary, thyme, and lavender dry well and retain useful flavour for cocktail syrups. Bundle stems and hang upside down in a warm, dry spot for 1–2 weeks. Store in an airtight jar out of direct light. Dried mint and basil lose most of what makes them interesting in a drink — better to make syrup than dry those.

Six techniques worth knowing

How to Turn Herbs Into Drinks

There are a handful of techniques that cover almost everything a cocktail herb can do. None of them require special equipment. Master these and you can build a drink around almost any herb in the garden. All our recipes use at least one of these methods.

🍯
Simple Syrups
The most useful way to use cocktail herbs. Simmer equal parts sugar and water, steep the herbs off heat for 15–30 minutes, strain and refrigerate. The syrup carries the herb's flavour into the drink without adding plant matter. Works with every herb on this page.
→ Best for: Lavender, rosemary, thyme, mint, lemon balm
🫙
Infusions
Pack fresh or dried herbs into a bottle of spirit and leave them for 24–72 hours at room temperature, tasting as you go. Strain when the flavour is where you want it. Cold infusions (in the fridge) take longer but are more delicate. Rosemary-infused gin and thyme-infused vodka are both worth making.
→ Best for: Rosemary, thyme, lavender, basil
🌿
Muddling
Press herbs gently in the bottom of a glass or shaker with a muddler or the back of a spoon to release their oils. The key word is gently — crushing too hard releases bitter compounds from the stems and seeds. Muddling works best with tender, leafy herbs. Avoid muddling rosemary or thyme stems; strip the leaves first.
→ Best for: Mint, basil, lemon balm
🧊
Herbal Ice Cubes
Freeze whole herb leaves or small sprigs in water in an ice cube tray. As the cubes melt, they release colour and a subtle flavour into the drink. They also look beautiful — especially mint, basil, or edible flowers. These work especially well in large-format drinks and pitchers where you want visual impact.
→ Best for: Mint, basil, lemon balm, borage flowers
🌸
Garnishes
A fresh herb garnish does two things: it adds aroma (guests smell the herb before they taste the drink) and it signals what's in the glass. Slap the herb between your palms before placing it — this releases the oils and wakes up the aroma immediately. Place it where the drinker's nose will pass over it as they sip.
→ Best for: Mint, rosemary, thyme, lavender sprigs
🍋
Mocktails
In non-alcoholic drinks, herbs carry more of the flavour load — and that's a feature, not a problem. Use herb syrups as the primary flavour component, lean on citrus and sparkling water for structure, and treat the herb garnish seriously. Lemon balm, mint, and basil make particularly exceptional alcohol-free drinks. See our full recipe collection.
→ Best for: Lemon balm, mint, basil, lavender

Common questions

Frequently Asked Questions

Mint is the easiest cocktail herb to grow — it's almost indestructible, grows fast, and comes back every year. The only rule: always grow it in a container, as it spreads aggressively underground. Basil is the second easiest, though it needs warmth and dislikes cold nights.

Yes, several cocktail herbs grow well indoors. Mint, basil, and lemon balm do best on a bright windowsill with at least 6 hours of light. Rosemary and thyme can work indoors but prefer a sunny south-facing window or a grow light. Lavender is difficult indoors and is best grown outside or in a container you can bring in seasonally.

Mint and basil work well together in citrus-forward drinks. Rosemary and thyme complement each other in spirit-forward cocktails. Lavender pairs beautifully with lemon balm for floral, gentle drinks. For a classic garden combination, thyme and lemon verbena work exceptionally well with gin and light spirits.

Regular harvesting keeps herbs productive — even if you don't need them. For basil, harvest every 1 to 2 weeks and pinch flower buds as soon as they appear. Mint can be cut hard every few weeks and will bounce back quickly. Rosemary and thyme are slower-growing; harvest stems as needed without taking more than a third of the plant at once.

Freezing works best when you turn herbs into something first. Freeze herb-infused simple syrups in ice cube trays for a ready-to-use bar ingredient. You can also freeze whole mint or basil leaves in water to make herb ice cubes — they look beautiful in drinks. Freezing bare leaves tends to make them limp and discoloured, so it's better for cooking than for garnishes.

Fresh herbs are brighter, more aromatic, and better for garnishes, muddling, and fast infusions. Dried herbs are more concentrated — use about half the quantity — and work well in syrups where you're extracting flavour over heat. Dried lavender and rosemary make excellent syrups. Dried mint and basil lose most of what makes them interesting in a drink.

Not at all. A few pots on a sunny balcony or windowsill is enough to keep a home bar stocked with fresh herbs all season. Mint, basil, thyme, and lemon balm all grow happily in containers. A half-dozen pots with different herbs will give you more than you can use.

Mint, lemon balm, and basil are the best mocktail herbs — they bring brightness and complexity without needing alcohol to express their flavour. Lemon balm in particular shines in non-alcoholic drinks because its gentle citrus notes don't need a spirit to anchor them. Lavender also works beautifully in sparkling mocktails with lemon or elderflower.

Bolting happens when herbs flower — triggered by heat, long days, or stress. Once a herb flowers, the leaves turn bitter and the plant focuses energy on seeds rather than foliage. Prevent it by harvesting regularly, pinching flower buds off as soon as they appear (especially basil), and keeping plants well-watered in hot weather. Growing herbs in partial shade during the hottest part of summer also helps delay bolting.
