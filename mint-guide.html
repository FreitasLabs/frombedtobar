Cocktail Herb Guide

How to Grow Mint for
Cocktails & Mocktails

Mint is the most useful herb in a bar garden. It grows fast, thrives in containers, comes back every year, and makes better drinks than anything you'll find at the supermarket. This is everything you need to know — from soil to shaker.

Why Grow Mint?

Most cocktail herbs take patience. Mint is not one of them. It establishes quickly, produces heavily, and earns its place in the bar garden faster than anything else you'll plant. Here's why it's the first herb most home bartenders should grow. For a full comparison of all cocktail herbs, see the Cocktail Herb Guide.

🌱
Beginner-friendly
Mint is one of the most forgiving plants you can grow. It tolerates under-watering, over-watering, inconsistent attention, and hard cutting. If you kill mint, you've worked at it. Most beginners find it's the one herb they can't keep up with rather than the one they struggle to keep alive.
⚡
Fast-growing
A single nursery plant can produce enough for regular use within a few weeks. Unlike slow-growing herbs such as rosemary, mint is productive almost immediately and keeps going — a well-established plant can be cut to a few inches and recover fully in 3 to 4 weeks.
🪴
Perfect for containers
Mint is actually better in a container than in the ground. It's easier to manage, easier to move, and a pot prevents the aggressive spreading that makes open-ground mint such a problem. A 12-inch pot on a sunny step or balcony is all you need to keep a bar stocked all season.
🍹
Exceptional flavour in drinks
Supermarket mint is cut days or weeks before you use it. The essential oils that give mint its brightness — the same compounds that make a mojito taste alive — degrade rapidly after cutting. Garden-fresh mint in a drink is noticeably different. It's not a subtle improvement.
🔄
Comes back every year
Plant once, harvest for years. Mint is a perennial that dies back to the roots in winter and regrows in spring — in zones 3 through 11. Unlike annual herbs you need to resow each year, a pot of mint is a one-time investment that pays off indefinitely.
🫗
Brilliant in mocktails
Mint is arguably the single best mocktail herb. In non-alcoholic drinks it provides the freshness and complexity that a spirit would otherwise contribute. Mint lemonade, mint iced tea, and sparkling mint agua fresca are genuinely satisfying drinks that don't feel like a compromise. Browse our full recipe collection.

Which Mint Variety is Best for Drinks?

Not all mint is the same. The variety you choose affects flavour, intensity, and how well it holds up when muddled or infused. These four are the most useful for cocktails and mocktails.

Spearmint
Best all-rounder
Flavour
Bright, sweet, and clean with mild menthol. The most balanced and versatile cocktail mint.
Best used for
Muddling, syrups, garnish — works in virtually every mint drink.
Drinks
Mojito, Mint Julep, Mint Lemonade, Southside
Mojito Mint
Best for muddling
Flavour
A spearmint cultivar with larger, softer leaves and a cleaner finish. Less medicinal than standard spearmint.

Muddling — the larger leaves bruise easily and release flavour without bitterness.
Drinks

Peppermint
Use with care
Flavour
High menthol, sharp, and intensely cooling. Much more assertive than spearmint — a little goes a long way.
Best used for
Syrups only — too intense for muddling unless deliberately going for a strong mint flavour.

Drinks
Mint chocolate martini, hot chocolate mocktail, garnish for espresso drinks

In the garden

Growing Mint

Mint is hard to kill, but it does have preferences. Give it the right conditions and it'll produce more than you can use. Get the container rule wrong and you'll spend years trying to reclaim your garden. For zone-specific planting timing, see the planting guide.

What mint needs

  • Sun: 4–6 hours of direct sun per day. Mint tolerates partial shade better than most herbs — afternoon shade in hot climates actually helps prevent bolting.
  • Soil: Rich, moist, well-draining potting mix. Mint prefers more moisture than woody herbs like rosemary. Don't let it dry out completely between waterings.
  • Water: Keep soil consistently moist. In containers, this may mean watering every 1–2 days in summer. Wilting leaves that perk up after watering are a sign it's thirsty rather than diseased.
  • Container size: A minimum of 12 inches wide and deep. Larger is better — mint roots spread horizontally and a small pot will become root-bound quickly.
  • Drainage: Essential. Mint likes moisture but not standing water. Always use a pot with drainage holes.
  • Feeding: A liquid feed once a month during the growing season keeps production high. Mint is not particularly demanding, but regular feeding noticeably improves leaf size and vigour.

The container rule — and why it matters

  • Always grow mint in a container. This is not optional. Mint spreads via underground runners that can travel several feet per season. Once it's in open ground, it's almost impossible to remove.
  • If you want it in a bed: Bury a container (with the bottom cut out) so the rim sits 2 inches above soil level. This blocks runners without fully restricting the roots.
  • Repot every 2–3 years. Mint roots become crowded and the plant loses vigour. In early spring, divide the root mass and repot into fresh compost.
  • One pot per variety. Different mint varieties will cross-pollinate if grown together over time, and the flavour of hybrid offspring is often inferior.
  • Overwinter: Cut the plant back hard in autumn and move it somewhere sheltered. It will die back and re-emerge in spring. In severe climates, bring containers inside to a cool, bright spot.

From garden to glass

How to Harvest Mint

How you harvest mint affects both the quality of the ingredient and the long-term productivity of the plant. These four steps cover everything that matters.

01 — When

Harvest in the morning

Essential oils — the compounds that give mint its brightness in a drink — are most concentrated in the morning before heat causes them to evaporate. If you're making a batch cocktail or syrup for an evening, cut your mint that morning and keep it in a glass of water like cut flowers. The difference between morning-cut and afternoon-cut mint in a drink is noticeable.

02 — How

Cut above a leaf node

Use clean scissors to cut stems just above a pair of leaves — the point where two leaves meet the stem. The plant will branch out from that node and become bushier rather than just growing taller. Avoid tearing or pulling, which can damage the stem. For garnishes, choose the newest, most tender stems at the top of the plant — these have the best colour and aroma.

03 — How much

Take up to a third at once

Removing more than a third of the plant at one time stresses it and slows recovery. For regular cocktail use, take individual stems as needed. If you need a large harvest for a batch syrup or a party, cut the whole plant back by about half — it will recover fully within 2 to 3 weeks. A full cut-back to 3 inches above soil level is also healthy to do once or twice per season.

04 — Growth

Pinch flowers immediately

As soon as you see flower buds forming, pinch them off. Flowering (bolting) is triggered by heat and long days, and once a mint plant flowers, the leaves turn bitter and the plant's energy goes into seed production rather than new foliage. Regular harvesting naturally delays flowering by mimicking the plant's natural growth cycle. In midsummer, a hard cut-back will reset the plant and produce a fresh flush of flavourful new growth.

Making it last

How to Preserve Mint

A productive mint plant will give you more than you can use fresh. These five methods turn a summer surplus into a year-round bar ingredient.

❄️
Refrigeration
Store cut mint stems in a glass with an inch of water, loosely covered with a plastic bag, in the fridge. This keeps mint fresh for 5 to 7 days — far longer than in a sealed bag, where it tends to turn black within 2 days.
→ Best for fresh garnishes and muddling use within the week
🍯
Mint Simple Syrup
Bring equal parts water and sugar to a simmer, remove from heat, steep a large handful of fresh mint for 20 minutes, strain and bottle. Keeps in the fridge for 2 to 3 weeks. The most useful way to preserve mint — a spoonful in sparkling water is a drink in itself.
→ Best for batch cocktails, mocktails, and everyday use
🧊
Herb Ice Cubes
Freeze whole mint leaves or stems in water in an ice cube tray. As the cubes melt in a drink they release colour and a subtle mint flavour. Visually striking in long drinks and pitchers. Alternatively, freeze mint syrup in an ice cube tray for a ready-measured bar ingredient.
→ Best for drinks presentation and batch-prep convenience
🌡️
Drying
Bundle mint stems and hang upside down in a warm, dry spot for 1 to 2 weeks. Dried mint loses the bright, fresh character that makes it useful in cocktails — it's not recommended for muddling or garnish. Use dried mint only in hot drink syrups where you're heating and extracting, not relying on freshness.
→ Only useful for hot drink syrups — make syrup instead where possible
🫙
Mint-Infused Spirit
Pack fresh mint loosely into a jar of vodka or white rum and leave at room temperature for 12 to 24 hours, tasting as it steeps. Strain when the flavour is right — don't over-infuse or it turns bitter. The resulting spirit keeps for months and adds mint flavour without adding sweetness.
→ Best for spirit-forward cocktails and batch mixing

In the glass

Using Mint in Drinks

Mint works in five distinct ways in a drink. Understanding what each technique does — and what it's for — makes you a significantly better home bartender. See Bar Essentials for tool recommendations, and Recipes for full drink recipes using mint.

🥃
Muddling
Press mint leaves gently in the bottom of a glass or shaker with a muddler or the back of a spoon to release their oils. The word is gently — over-muddling crushes stems and seeds, releasing bitter chlorophyll. Use leaves only, not stems, and press rather than grind. 4 to 6 leaves is usually enough for a single drink.
→ Best variety: Spearmint or Mojito Mint
🌿
Garnish
A fresh mint sprig placed where the drinker's nose will pass over it — typically sitting above the straw or on the rim — adds powerful aroma before the first sip. Slap the sprig between your palms before placing it: this ruptures some of the cells and releases the essential oils. A wilted or discoloured sprig does the opposite of what it should.
→ Choose fresh, vivid green stems from the top of the plant
🍯
Simple Syrups
Mint syrup carries the herb's flavour cleanly into a drink without adding plant matter. It also sweetens, which means you can adjust the sugar and mint levels independently when building a recipe. Equal parts water and sugar, off the heat, steeped for 15 to 20 minutes. Don't boil the mint — heat destroys the delicate aromatics.
→ Best variety: Spearmint. Never boil — steep only
🫙
Infusions
Infusing mint into a spirit — vodka or white rum work best — extracts flavour without sweetness, giving you more control in a drink. Cold infusions (in the fridge) take longer but are more delicate and less likely to become bitter. Taste every few hours and strain when it's where you want it. Never infuse for more than 24 hours.
→ Best for spirit-forward drinks where you want control over sweetness
🍋
Mocktails
In non-alcoholic drinks, mint is the single most effective herb for providing complexity. A mint syrup with fresh citrus juice and sparkling water is a complete, satisfying drink. For mocktails, use a slightly more generous hand with the mint — without alcohol to carry the flavour, the herb needs to work harder. See our recipes for full mocktail versions of every drink.
→ Increase syrup quantity by about 25% vs cocktail versions
🧊
Herb Ice Cubes
Freeze mint leaves in water or mint syrup in ice cube trays. In a long drink, the cubes melt slowly and release flavour as the drink progresses — the mint presence actually increases over time rather than diminishing. Visually, a clear cube with a bright green leaf suspended inside is a simple, striking presentation detail that takes no skill.
→ Use silicone moulds for clearest results

From the garden

Best Mint Drink Ideas

These are the drinks that show mint at its best — from the classics to the alcohol-free. Browse our full recipe collection for complete recipes, or visit the Cocktail Herb Guide for pairing ideas across all herbs.

Mojito
Cocktail
The defining mint cocktail. White rum, fresh lime juice, mint leaves, sugar syrup, and soda water. Mint is muddled first — gently — and the whole drink is built in the glass. The quality of the mint matters more than the quality of the rum. Spearmint or mojito mint only.
Spirit: White rum · Mocktail: sparkling water + extra lime
Mint Julep
Cocktail
Bourbon, mint syrup, and crushed ice in a silver cup. One of the best demonstrations of why homegrown mint is worth the effort — a mint julep built with garden-fresh syrup is completely different to one made with shop-bought mint. The aroma is the drink.
Spirit: Bourbon · Mocktail: strong brewed tea + mint syrup + crushed ice
Mint Smash
Cocktail
Muddled mint and lemon with a spirit of your choice, shaken with ice and strained. Simpler than it sounds and more impressive than it looks. Works with almost any white spirit — gin, vodka, or white rum. Adjust the lemon-to-sweetness ratio to taste.
Spirit: Gin or vodka · Mocktail: apple juice base + muddled mint
Mint Lemonade
Mocktail / Cocktail
Fresh lemon juice, mint syrup, and sparkling water over ice. One of the best mint mocktails — fully satisfying without feeling like an afterthought. Spike it with vodka if you want a cocktail, or add elderflower cordial for a more complex non-alcoholic version.
Spirit: Vodka optional · Mocktail: stand-alone, excellent as-is
Mint Iced Tea
Mocktail / Cocktail
Cold-brewed black tea with mint syrup and a wedge of lemon. Cold-brew the tea overnight rather than hot-steeping — it produces a smoother, less bitter base that works better in a drink. Add bourbon for a long, low-alcohol cocktail version that works well in early autumn.
Spirit: Bourbon (optional) · Mocktail: naturally excellent without alcohol
Garden Spritz
Cocktail / Mocktail
A light, herbal spritz built on mint syrup, fresh cucumber slices, and sparkling wine or sparkling water. The cucumber softens the mint's intensity and makes this feel green and garden-forward. A summer party drink that non-drinkers and cocktail lovers both want a second glass of.
Spirit: Prosecco or gin · Mocktail: sparkling water + cucumber rounds

What to avoid

Common Mint Mistakes

Mint is forgiving, but a few mistakes show up repeatedly — both in the garden and at the bar. These are the ones worth knowing before you start.

Mistake 01
Planting mint in an open garden bed
Mint spreads aggressively via underground runners. In open ground it will colonise surrounding beds within a season and is extremely difficult to eradicate once established.
Fix: Always grow in a container, or use the buried-pot method described in the growing section above.
Mistake 02
Over-muddling
Pressing too hard or too long when muddling crushes stems and seeds along with the leaves, releasing bitter chlorophyll and green plant compounds that make the drink taste harsh rather than fresh.
Fix: Press gently 4 to 5 times. You want bruised, not pulverised. Use leaves only — strip them from stems first.
Mistake 03
Letting mint flower
Once mint flowers (bolts), the leaves turn bitter and the plant dedicates its energy to seed production rather than new, flavourful growth. This happens faster in heat and when the plant is under-harvested.
Fix: Pinch off flower buds as soon as they appear. Harvest regularly to delay bolting. Cut the plant back hard at the first sign of flowering.
Mistake 04
Harvesting too aggressively at once
Removing more than half the plant in one session slows recovery significantly. An over-stripped mint plant can take 4 to 6 weeks to regrow, leaving you without a harvest when you need it.
Fix: Take no more than a third of the plant at once. If you need a large harvest, give the plant 3 weeks to recover before cutting again.
Mistake 05
Using dried mint in cocktails
Dried mint loses the volatile aromatics that make fresh mint so effective in drinks. It works in some hot-drink contexts but in cold cocktails it contributes little except a slightly musty herb flavour.
Fix: Make and freeze a mint syrup from fresh leaves when you have surplus. This preserves far more of the useful flavour than drying.
Mistake 06
Wrong location for the pot
Mint in a pot placed in full afternoon sun in a hot climate can dry out completely within a day. A stressed, dehydrated mint plant produces bitter, small-leafed growth that's poor in drinks.
Fix: Choose a spot with morning sun and afternoon shade in warm climates. Keep the pot in a saucer of water during heatwaves to provide bottom-up moisture.
