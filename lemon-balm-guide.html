Cocktail Herb Guide

How to Grow Lemon Balm for
Cocktails & Mocktails

Lemon balm is the underestimated herb — the one that produces abundantly, asks for almost nothing, and makes drinks feel lighter and more alive than they did without it. Gentle, citrus-mint, and exceptionally kind to mocktails, it belongs in every bar garden. The secret is in the cold infusion: this is an herb that reveals itself in cold water, not hot.

Why Grow Lemon Balm?

Most bar garden herbs demand something from you — the right soil, the right temperature, the right pruning timing. Lemon balm asks almost nothing and gives back generously. For the full picture on cocktail herbs, see the Cocktail Herb Guide.

🫗
The mocktail herb
Lemon balm's gentle, citrus-floral character shines most brightly in alcohol-free drinks — it provides the aromatic complexity that a spirit would otherwise supply without any heaviness or bitterness. A lemon balm cold infusion in sparkling water with a squeeze of lemon and a spoonful of honey is one of the finest non-alcoholic garden drinks there is.
❄️
Cold infusion extraordinaire
Lemon balm is one of the rare herbs that is better cold-infused than hot. Packing fresh leaves into cold water or spirits and refrigerating overnight produces a clean, fragrant result that heat can't match — hot water destroys the delicate volatile compounds that give lemon balm its distinctiveness. This one technique unlocks the herb's full potential.
🌿
Extraordinarily productive
Lemon balm grows vigorously and produces enormous quantities of harvestable leaves from a single plant. Where other herbs give you enough for one or two drinks per week, a mature lemon balm plant gives you enough to cold-infuse a whole pitcher, make a batch of syrup, and still have garnish left over — all from a few stems.
🧊
Works from spring to first frost
Unlike basil (summer only) or lavender (a few weeks in July), lemon balm produces from early spring right through to the first hard frost in fall — often six or seven months of usable material. It's one of the longest-season herbs in the bar garden, making it as useful in a chilly April spritz as in an August lemonade.
🤝
The great companion herb
Lemon balm plays well with almost everything. Mixed with mint it adds citrus brightness. With lavender it becomes more floral. With cucumber it produces one of the most refreshing flavoured waters imaginable. It's the herb that makes other herbs better — a quiet amplifier in the bar garden.
🌍
Grows almost anywhere
Lemon balm is one of the most adaptable plants in the bar garden — it tolerates partial shade, average soil, northern climates (hardy to zone 3), and occasional neglect with equal equanimity. If you have a spot that's too shady for lavender or too cold for basil, lemon balm will thrive there. Browse our full recipe collection for ideas.

Which Lemon Balm Variety is Best for Drinks?

The variety range for lemon balm is narrower than for mint or basil — the species (Melissa officinalis) is fairly consistent across cultivars. There are a few worth knowing about.

Growth

In the ground

How to Grow Lemon Balm

Lemon balm is one of the easiest plants in the bar garden. The only real task is containment — without it, you'll have more lemon balm than you can use and it will be growing where you don't want it.

What lemon balm needs

  • Sun: Full sun to partial shade. Full sun produces the most fragrant leaves; partial shade produces a larger, lusher plant with a slightly lighter flavour. Both work well for bar use.
  • Soil: Tolerates a wide range of soil conditions — average to moderately fertile, well-draining. Unlike Mediterranean herbs, it doesn't require lean, gritty soil and does well in typical garden soil.
  • Water: Moderate and consistent. More moisture-tolerant than lavender or rosemary but still prefers not to be waterlogged. Check container-grown plants regularly in hot weather.
  • Containment: Non-negotiable. Lemon balm spreads by underground runners exactly like mint. Use a container, or sink a pot into the border with the rim above soil level. This is the most important growing decision you'll make with this plant.
  • Cold hardiness: Exceptionally cold-hardy — reliably perennial to zone 3. Dies back to the ground in winter and regrows strongly in spring. One of the most winter-tough cocktail herbs available.
  • Feeding: Light feeding in spring encourages strong new growth. Avoid heavy nitrogen feeding which produces lush but less fragrant foliage.

Common growing problems

  • Spreading beyond its allotted space
    The single most common lemon balm problem. An uncontained plant will spread several feet in all directions within a couple of seasons. It's vigorous, determined, and difficult to fully eradicate once established in a border.
  • Allowing it to self-seed
    Lemon balm produces abundant seed if allowed to flower. The seeds germinate readily, producing new plants throughout the garden. Cut flower stems before they set seed to prevent unwanted seedlings.
  • Overwatering in containers
    Container-grown lemon balm is more susceptible to waterlogging than in-ground plants. Use a pot with good drainage holes and don't let it sit in standing water. In cool, wet weather, ease off watering significantly.
  • Harvesting too infrequently
    Unlike more delicate herbs, lemon balm actually improves with regular cutting. Infrequent harvesting leads to a large, coarse plant with woody stems and older, less fragrant leaves. Cut it back regularly and the resulting growth is young, aromatic, and productive.
The containment method that works

The most reliable containment approach is a bottomless bucket or large pot sunk into the border, with the rim sitting about 1 inch above the soil surface. This stops underground runners escaping into the surrounding bed while keeping the plant looking like part of the border rather than a container. Check the rim every spring — lemon balm can jump the rim if the soil level outside rises to meet it.

From the plant

When and How to Harvest

Lemon balm is harvestable almost continuously from early spring through to the first frost. A few principles make the harvest more fragrant and keep the plant producing well all season.

Step 01

Always harvest young tip growth

The most aromatic and flavourful part of the plant is the young, bright green tip growth at the ends of the stems. Older leaves lower down the plant are larger but coarser in texture and lighter in fragrance. For cold infusions and cocktail garnishes, use only the young leaves — this also encourages bushy new growth from below the cut.

Step 02

Harvest before and during warm weather

Lemon balm is most fragrant in warm, sunny weather when the essential oils in the leaves are most concentrated. Harvest in the morning after any dew has dried but before the midday heat. In cool or overcast conditions the fragrance is noticeably lighter — if you're making a syrup or cold infusion for a special occasion, try to harvest on a warm, sunny day.

Step 03

Pinch flowers to extend the leaf season

Lemon balm flowers in midsummer — small white or pale pink blooms that attract pollinators but signal the plant is shifting energy away from leaf production. Pinch flower spikes before they open to extend the productive leaf harvest. The flowers are edible and make delicate garnishes, but let too many open and the remaining leaf flavour coarsens slightly.

Step 04

Cut back hard once or twice a season

Every 6 to 8 weeks, cut the whole plant back by about half — right back into the young growth above the older, woodier stems. This prevents the plant becoming tall and leggy, encourages a fresh flush of young aromatic leaves, and keeps the harvest quality high all season. Lemon balm responds enthusiastically to hard cutting and recovers within 2 weeks.

Step 05

Use fresh immediately or cold-infuse

Fresh lemon balm wilts fairly quickly once cut — use it for garnishes and cold infusions on the day of harvest. For cold infusions, pack the leaves into cold water or spirits immediately after cutting and refrigerate. The cold extraction begins working right away and produces its best results after 4 to 8 hours. Don't wait — freshly harvested leaves produce the most fragrant infusions.

Step 06

Make a pre-frost harvest for syrup

Before the first frost of fall, make a final large harvest and turn it into lemon balm syrup — then freeze in ice cube trays. Unlike lavender or rosemary (which dry well), lemon balm is best preserved as a frozen syrup. One good fall harvest gives you lemon balm cocktails through winter from your own garden.

Make it last

Preserving Your Lemon Balm Harvest

Lemon balm doesn't dry as well as rosemary or thyme — the delicate volatile compounds that make it distinctive are largely destroyed by heat and air drying. Freeze it as syrup, cold-infuse it fresh, or use it within a day of harvest for best results.

❄️
Cold Water Infusion
Pack a large jug or jar with fresh lemon balm leaves — as many as will fit — and cover with cold water. Refrigerate for 4 to 8 hours or overnight. The resulting flavoured water is one of the finest non-alcoholic garden drinks available: clean, fragrant, and distinctly lemon-herbal. Strain and serve over ice with a fresh leaf garnish.
Add cucumber slices, fresh mint, or a few lavender sprigs to the jug for a more complex, spa-quality flavoured water.
🫙
Lemon Balm Syrup
Equal parts sugar and water, dissolved over gentle heat. Remove from heat, add a very large handful of fresh leaves (30 to 40), and steep for 25 to 35 minutes. The flavour is subtle — use more leaves than you think you need and don't be impatient with the steeping time. Strain, cool, and refrigerate. Alternatively, cold-infuse the syrup overnight for a cleaner result.
Keeps 2 weeks refrigerated. The cold-infusion method (leaves steeped in already-cooled syrup overnight) produces a noticeably more fragrant result than hot steeping.
🧊
Syrup Ice Cubes
Freeze finished lemon balm syrup in ice cube trays for up to 3 months. The best way to extend lemon balm's flavour beyond the growing season — drop a cube into a glass of sparkling water with a squeeze of lemon for an instant, season-extending garden drink. Or use directly in a shaker for a Lemon Balm Collins or gin fizz.
Lemon balm syrup cubes and lemon thyme syrup cubes look identical — label both trays clearly on the day of freezing.
🫗
Cold Spirit Infusion
Pack fresh lemon balm leaves into a jar and cover with gin, vodka, or light rum. Seal and refrigerate for 6 to 12 hours — cold infusion takes longer than room-temperature steeping but produces a cleaner, more delicate flavour. Taste at intervals; the infusion is ready when the spirit smells clearly of lemon balm without being grassy or bitter. Strain and use within 2 months.
Gin cold-infused with lemon balm is exceptional — the botanical overlap creates something that tastes considered and complete. Try it in a simple gin and tonic.
🍯
Lemon Balm Honey
Warm honey gently (don't boil) and steep 15 to 20 fresh leaves for 20 to 30 minutes. The lemon note in the honey is subtle — more of a fragrant whisper than a pronounced flavour. Works best where honey is already the sweetener and you want a gentle herbal lift. Stir into sparkling water with a squeeze of lemon for the simplest possible garden mocktail.
Keeps for several months at room temperature. Lemon balm honey and fresh lemon in sparkling water is a drink that needs no other introduction.
🌿
Drying (Limited Use)
Lemon balm can be dried but loses most of its delicate citrus notes in the process — what remains is a more generic herby flavour without the distinctive lemon-mint character. Dried lemon balm works adequately in strongly sweetened syrups where you need the bulk rather than the subtlety, but for anything where the flavour should be the point, fresh or frozen is always preferable.
If drying, do so quickly at low temperature — a dehydrator at 95°F preserves more of the volatile oils than air drying or oven drying.

At the bar

Using Lemon Balm in Drinks

Lemon balm's greatest quality at the bar is its lightness — it makes drinks feel fresher and more alive without adding weight or complication. These are the techniques that get the most from it.

❄️
Cold Infusion
The definitive lemon balm bar technique. Pack fresh leaves into cold water, spirits, or cooled simple syrup and refrigerate for 4 to 12 hours. The result is far more fragrant and distinctly lemon-herbal than any hot preparation. Use cold-infused water as the base for mocktails, or cold-infused gin or vodka as the spirit in cocktails.
Use a generous quantity of leaves — lemon balm's flavour is delicate and a too-sparse infusion produces barely flavoured water. Fill the container at least two-thirds with leaves before adding liquid.
🫙
Simple Syrup
The most practical way to use lemon balm in mixed drinks. Make the syrup with more leaves than you think you need and steep longer rather than shorter — the flavour is delicate and needs generous extraction. Use in gin and tonic, a Collins, sparkling lemonade, or anywhere a light citrus-herbal note is welcome.
The cold-infusion syrup method produces a noticeably better result: make simple syrup, let it cool completely, add fresh leaves, and refrigerate overnight before straining. Worth the extra time.
🌿
Garnish
A few fresh lemon balm leaves floated on the surface of a clear drink or layered inside a tall glass are a clean, bright garnish that signals freshness without the visual weight of a large herb sprig. The leaves are attractive in their own right — slightly textured, bright green, and fragrant enough to deliver scent with each sip.
Slap the leaves lightly before placing to release the fragrance. Lemon balm releases its oils more readily than most herbs — even a light press is enough.
🧉
Muddling
Lemon balm muddles cleanly and easily — the soft leaves release their oils with minimal pressure. Use 8 to 10 young leaves, press gently 3 to 4 times, and proceed directly to adding ice and spirits. The result is a clean, lemon-herbal note that works particularly well in gin sours and vodka drinks. Always double-strain for a clean finished cocktail.
Lemon balm and cucumber muddled together is one of the most refreshing combinations in the summer bar garden — try it with gin and elderflower for a drink that tastes like a garden in a glass.
🤝
Blend with Other Herbs
Lemon balm is one of the best companion herbs at the bar — it amplifies and lifts whatever it's paired with. Lemon balm and mint produces a citrus-fresh variation on standard mint preparations. Lemon balm and lavender is a floral-citrus combination that's exceptional in a gin spritz. Lemon balm and cucumber in cold water is one of the great warm-weather non-alcoholic drinks.
When combining lemon balm with stronger herbs like lavender or rosemary, use roughly twice the lemon balm to one part the assertive herb — otherwise the lemon balm disappears entirely.
🫧
Flavoured Water
The simplest and most directly satisfying use of lemon balm. A large jug of cold-infused lemon balm water — served over ice with a squeeze of lemon and a few fresh leaves — is a drink that looks and tastes like someone put real thought into it, made from nothing but water and what's in the garden. For the bar table or a summer gathering, it's the drink that empties the jug first.
Make it the day before — 12 hours of cold infusion produces a noticeably more fragrant result than 2 hours. A jug in the fridge overnight is ready exactly when you need it for the next day.

In the glass

What to Make with Garden Lemon Balm

Lemon balm's best drinks are clean, light, and built around its citrus-floral character. Start with the cold infusion and build from there.

Lemon Balm Collins
Cocktail · Classic Build
Gin or vodka, fresh lemon juice, lemon balm syrup, and sparkling water — built long over ice with a few fresh lemon balm leaves. The Collins is the ideal framework for lemon balm: the citrus base aligns perfectly with the herb's lemon-mint character, and the sparkling water lifts the whole thing into something that feels genuinely refreshing.
Spirit: London Dry Gin or Vodka  ·  Syrup: Cold-infused lemon balm
Lemon Balm Gin Fizz
Cocktail · Summer
Gin, fresh lemon juice, lemon balm syrup, and a splash of soda — shaken hard and strained into a chilled glass. Sharper and more concentrated than the Collins, this is a drink where the lemon balm's delicacy is showcased rather than diluted. Use a lemon-forward gin for the best result — the citrus botanicals in the spirit and the citrus note of the herb amplify each other.
Spirit: Lemon-forward Gin  ·  Garnish: 3 fresh lemon balm leaves floated
Melissa Spritz
Cocktail · Aperitif
Lemon balm-infused gin or vodka, elderflower liqueur, prosecco, and a splash of sparkling water over ice. Melissa is the old common name for lemon balm (from the genus Melissa officinalis) and this drink honours it — the floral-citrus combination of lemon balm and elderflower is one of the most elegant in the bar garden. Light, fragrant, and exactly right before dinner.
Spirit: Lemon balm-infused Gin  ·  Liqueur: Elderflower
Lemon Balm Vodka Tonic
Cocktail · Simple Build
Lemon balm-infused vodka or plain vodka with lemon balm syrup and tonic — the simplest possible showcase for the herb's character. Vodka's neutrality means nothing competes with the lemon balm. Use a good-quality tonic with natural quinine bitterness; the bitterness of the tonic contrasts beautifully with the lemon balm's sweetness.
Spirit: Lemon balm-infused Vodka or plain Vodka  ·  Tonic: Natural quinine tonic
Lemon Balm Sparkling Lemonade
Mocktail · The House Favourite
Fresh lemon juice, lemon balm syrup, sparkling water, and ice — finished with 3 or 4 fresh lemon balm leaves. This is the drink that earns its place at the table alongside every cocktail. Fresh lemon and lemon balm are natural allies — the herb softens and rounds the lemon's sharpness while adding a floral complexity that makes the whole drink taste more interesting than the ingredients suggest.
Add honey instead of plain syrup for a rounder, more complex sweetness — or use lemon balm honey for both at once
Garden Cold Infusion Water
Mocktail · The Simplest Good Drink
A large jug packed with fresh lemon balm leaves, cucumber slices, and a few fresh mint sprigs — covered with cold water and refrigerated overnight. The result is a flavoured water that makes people ask what's in the jug. It's not complicated. It's not impressive-sounding. But it's one of the best things you can pour from the bar garden, and it costs almost nothing to make.
Add lavender, lemon thyme, or a handful of garden strawberries to vary the profile through the season

Common Lemon Balm Mistakes

Lemon balm is forgiving but a few habits limit what you can get from it at the bar.

Mistake 01
Not containing it
Planted freely in a border, lemon balm will colonise aggressively within two seasons. It's not dangerous, but it's persistent and hard to fully remove once established — the underground runners re-sprout from even small fragments left in the soil.
Fix: Always plant in a container or use the sunken-pot method in a border. This is not optional — it's the defining growing decision for this plant.
Mistake 02
Using hot infusion instead of cold
Hot water destroys the delicate volatile compounds that give lemon balm its lemon-floral character. A hot-steeped lemon balm syrup is noticeably less fragrant than a cold-infused one. Many people assume hot is always better for extraction — with lemon balm, it isn't.
Fix: Always cold-infuse when you want the clearest, most fragrant lemon balm flavour. Hot syrup still works but produces a more generic herbal result.
Mistake 03
Using too few leaves
Lemon balm's flavour is delicate — a sparse infusion produces barely flavoured water. Many people try a cold infusion with a few sprigs, find it disappointing, and write the herb off. The correct quantity is more than feels reasonable: pack the container generously.
Fix: Use at least 20 to 30 large leaves per 500ml of water or spirits. Fill the container at least two-thirds with leaves. Lemon balm can handle it.
Mistake 04
Harvesting old, coarse leaves
The large, dark green leaves lower down an old lemon balm stem are noticeably coarser in flavour than the bright young tip growth. Many people harvest whatever's closest to hand rather than selecting specifically for quality.
Fix: Always harvest from the stem tips — the bright, young, slightly crinkled leaves at the top of each stem. Leave the older lower leaves to sustain the plant.
Mistake 05
Trying to dry it
Dried lemon balm loses most of what makes it worth growing for cocktail use. The citrus volatiles evaporate during drying, leaving a less interesting herby flavour. Yet many guides recommend drying it because it's the standard herb preservation method.
Fix: Preserve lemon balm as frozen syrup, not dried herb. Make a batch of syrup, freeze in ice cube trays, and you'll have usable lemon balm flavour through winter with far more quality than any dried version can provide.
Mistake 06
Overlooking it for cocktails entirely
Most people grow lemon balm as a tea herb or a calming herb and never think of it in the context of a shaker or a glass. It sits in the border, gets cut back twice a season, and its bar potential goes completely unrealised.
Fix: The next time you walk past the lemon balm, grab a handful and put it in a jug of cold water. Refrigerate overnight. Try it the next day with sparkling water and a squeeze of lemon. That's the beginning of everything else.
