Growing for the Bar

Tips that actually matter

No gardening degree required. These are the things that make the difference between herbs you have to throw out and ingredients you actually use.

01
Start with herbs — they're the easiest win

Mint, basil, rosemary, and thyme are hard to kill, grow fast, and show up in more recipes than anything else you'll plant. If you're starting from nothing, start here. A few pots on a sunny porch is all you need.

Beginner
02
Pot your mint. Seriously.

Mint spreads underground and will take over any bed it shares. Plant it in its own container and keep it separate. This isn't optional advice — ask anyone who planted it in the ground once. The good news: it's basically indestructible in a pot. See the complete mint growing guide →

Important
03
Harvest before it flowers

When herbs bolt and flower, the leaves turn bitter. Pick regularly — even if you don't need it — to keep the plant productive. For basil especially, pinch the flower buds off as soon as they appear. The plant will focus energy back into the leaves.

Flavor tip
04
Make simple syrup while it's fresh

When you have more than you can use fresh, steep herbs in a hot simple syrup (equal parts sugar and water) and refrigerate. It keeps for 2–3 weeks and extends your harvest window significantly. Lavender, mint, rosemary, and thyme all work beautifully.

Bar hack
05
Freeze berries at peak season

Frozen berries muddle just as well as fresh and let you make summer recipes in October. Freeze them flat on a sheet pan first so they don't clump, then transfer to a bag. Strawberries, blueberries, and raspberries all freeze well for cocktail use.

Preservation
06
Plant perennials once, harvest for years

Rosemary, thyme, lemon thyme, and sage come back every year in most climates. They're the backbone of a bar garden because they're always there when you need them. Add a few lavender plants and you've got a permanent harvest cycle that takes almost no maintenance.

Long game
07
Grow edible flowers for garnish

Lavender, elderflower, borage (bright blue, tastes like cucumber), and nasturtiums are all edible and easy to grow. A single flower on a drink instantly elevates the presentation. They're also a great conversation starter at a backyard party.

Presentation
08
Morning is the best time to harvest

Herbs have the most essential oils — and the best flavor — in the morning before the heat of the day evaporates them. If you're making a batch cocktail or syrup for a party that evening, cut your herbs that morning and keep them in water like flowers until you're ready.
