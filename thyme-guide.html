Cocktail Herb Guide

How to Grow Thyme for
Cocktails & Mocktails

Thyme is the supporting actor that makes everything around it better. Earthy, delicate, and quietly complex — it doesn't shout like rosemary or announce itself like lavender. It just makes the drink taste more considered. And when thyme is in flower, it's one of the most beautiful things you can put in a glass.

Why Grow Thyme?

Every bar garden needs a herb that makes everything else work harder. Thyme is that herb — earthy and slightly floral, it supports and deepens other flavours without competing with them. It's also one of the most beautiful things in the garden when it flowers. For the full picture on cocktail herbs, see the Cocktail Herb Guide.

🎭
The best supporting herb
Thyme doesn't try to lead — it makes everything around it taste better. In a gin and tonic it adds a whisper of herbal earthiness. In a whiskey sour it deepens the botanical character. In a lemon mocktail it adds the complexity that would otherwise be missing. That quality of lifting rather than dominating is rare and valuable.
🌸
Flowers worth using
Thyme flowers in early summer and the blooms are tiny, delicate, and genuinely beautiful in a glass. A sprig of flowering thyme in a gin and tonic or sparkling mocktail is one of the most elegant garden garnishes you can produce — the kind that makes people reach for their phones before they reach for the drink.
🍋
Lemon thyme changes everything
Grow lemon thyme alongside common thyme and you have two distinct bar herbs from two small plants. Lemon thyme adds a bright, citrus-herb note that transforms gin drinks and pairs beautifully with tequila and grapefruit. It smells like someone distilled a lemon verbena and a thyme plant and poured them into the same bottle.
🧊
Works across all seasons
Unlike lavender's brief bloom window or basil's hard summer cutoff, thyme is harvestable for most of the year. In zones 5 and above it keeps most of its leaves through winter, meaning a light harvest is possible even on a cold January day when the rest of the bar garden is dormant.
🪴
Compact and container-friendly
Common thyme stays small — usually under 12 inches — which makes it one of the best herbs for windowsill growing, small pots, and limited-space gardens. A 6-inch pot of thyme on a sunny kitchen windowsill will supply enough for regular bar use all year.
🫗
Excellent in alcohol-free drinks
Thyme's earthy complexity means it doesn't disappear in a mocktail the way more delicate herbs sometimes do. Thyme lemonade, thyme honey soda, and thyme grapefruit spritz are drinks that feel genuinely considered rather than assembled. Browse our full recipe collection.

Which Thyme Variety is Best for Drinks?

Common thyme and lemon thyme are the two essential cocktail varieties — different enough to be worth growing separately, similar enough to share the same care requirements. The other varieties are interesting but secondary.

Common Thyme
Most versatile
Latin name
Thymus vulgaris
Flavour
Earthy, slightly floral, and warmly herbal. The base thyme flavour — clean and broad-spectrum.
Best for
Syrups, muddling, garnish — works in virtually every cocktail context.
Drinks
Thyme Whiskey Sour, Garden Herb Gimlet, Thyme G&T, Thyme Honey Lemonade
Lemon Thyme

Thymus citriodorus
Flavour
Bright citrus-herb — like lemon zest and thyme in one. More fragrant than common thyme, more herbal than lemon verbena.
Best for

Thymus vulgaris 'Silver Posie'
Flavour
Similar to common thyme but slightly lighter. The variegated silver-and-green foliage is the main attraction for bar use.
Best for
Garnish — the variegated leaves are strikingly beautiful in a glass and photograph well.

Grow alongside common thyme. Use silver thyme for garnish, common thyme for syrups and flavour.

Thymus serpyllum

Lighter than common thyme. Not worth growing specifically for cocktail use — the low, spreading habit produces short stems that are impractical for bar preparations.

Ground cover and path planting — beautiful between stepping stones where it releases fragrance when walked on, but not productive enough for the bar.

Common thyme or lemon thyme for bar use

In the ground

How to Grow Thyme

Thyme is one of the easiest Mediterranean herbs to grow well. Its needs are modest and it tolerates neglect better than almost anything else in the bar garden. Get the basics right and it largely looks after itself.

What thyme needs

  • Sun: Full sun is ideal — 6 or more hours per day. Thyme tolerates partial shade better than rosemary or lavender, but flavour and fragrance are noticeably better with more sun.
  • Drainage: Good drainage is essential. Like all Mediterranean herbs, thyme rots in waterlogged conditions. In heavy soil, add grit or grow in a container.
  • Watering: Minimal once established. Water when the top inch of soil is dry. Thyme actively prefers to dry out between waterings — consistent overwatering is the most common cause of death.
  • Soil: Tolerates poor, lean soil well. Rich, heavily amended soil produces lush but less fragrant foliage. If anything, erring toward a lean, gritty mix produces a more aromatic plant.
  • Pot size: Thyme is compact — a 6-inch pot is sufficient for a single plant, and a 10-inch pot will support two varieties side by side.
  • Cold hardiness: Hardy to zone 4 or 5 depending on variety. Common thyme is one of the more cold-tolerant cocktail herbs — more reliable in northern gardens than lavender or rosemary.

Common growing problems

  • Overwatering
    The most common way thyme dies in containers. It's often watered on the same schedule as more moisture-hungry plants — basil, tomatoes, mint — and steadily rots at the roots while looking fine above ground until it suddenly collapses.
  • Going woody prematurely
    Thyme naturally becomes woody at the base over time, but this happens faster when the plant isn't harvested regularly. Regular cutting of the soft tip growth keeps the plant producing new shoots and delays the woody phase.
  • Too much shade
    Thyme in insufficient sun grows soft, leggy, and with less fragrance. The flavour difference between sun-grown and shade-grown thyme in a syrup is noticeable. Even an extra hour or two of sun per day makes a difference.
  • Not replacing aging plants
    Thyme has a productive lifespan of 3 to 5 years before it becomes predominantly woody and unproductive. Many gardeners try to rehabilitate an old thyme plant when the easier answer is to take a cutting or buy a new plant.
Grow both common and lemon thyme together

Common thyme and lemon thyme have identical care requirements and complement each other perfectly at the bar. Two small pots side by side — or both varieties in a single larger container — give you the earthiness of common thyme for syrups and whiskey drinks, and the bright citrus note of lemon thyme for gin cocktails and lemonades. It's the most space-efficient double planting in the bar garden.

From the plant

When and How to Harvest

Thyme can be harvested almost year-round in most climates, but the flavour is best in the period leading up to and just after flowering. A few good habits make harvesting more productive and keep the plant in better shape for longer.

Step 01

Harvest the soft tip growth

The most aromatic and flavourful part of the plant is the soft, young tip growth — the last 2 to 3 inches of each stem. This is what to use for syrups, muddling, and fresh garnishes. Avoid cutting into the older, woody base stems, which have less flavour and won't regenerate well.

Step 02

Harvest frequently, not heavily

Regular light harvesting is better for the plant than occasional heavy cutting. Taking a few tips from multiple stems every week or two keeps the plant producing fresh growth continuously. Stripping half the plant at once stresses it and takes longer to recover — especially in hot weather.

Step 03

Harvest just before flowering for peak flavour

Like most herbs, the essential oil concentration in thyme is highest just before the plant flowers — the point at which the plant is putting maximum energy into its foliage. This pre-flower period is ideal for making syrup. Once the plant is in full flower, the leaf flavour softens slightly but the flowers themselves are usable.

Step 04

Collect flowering stems for garnishes

When thyme flowers in early summer, collect stems with their blooms intact for use as cocktail garnishes. A single sprig of flowering thyme in a gin and tonic or a sparkling mocktail is one of the most beautiful and distinctive bar garden garnishes of the whole growing year. Harvest these first and use them fresh the same day.

Step 05

Trim after flowering to encourage regrowth

After the main flowering period, trim the plant by about a third — cutting back the flowered tips into green growth. This removes the spent flower heads, prevents the plant from putting energy into seed production, and encourages a fresh flush of aromatic leaf growth for summer and fall harvests.

Step 06

Use fresh or dry immediately

Fresh thyme can be stored loosely wrapped in a damp cloth in the fridge for 3 to 5 days. For longer storage, dry small bundles hanging in a warm ventilated space — they'll be ready in about a week. Thyme dries better than many herbs and retains its flavour well for 6 to 9 months in a sealed jar.

Make it last

Preserving Your Thyme Harvest

Thyme is one of the more versatile herbs to preserve for bar use. It dries reliably, infuses cleanly, and makes excellent syrups that freeze well for year-round use.

🌾
Drying
Bundle small amounts of thyme loosely and hang in a warm, dark, well-ventilated space for 1 week. Thyme dries quickly and evenly. Strip the dried leaves from the stems into a sealed jar — they keep their flavour well for 6 to 9 months and can be used directly in syrups and infusions.
Dried thyme is proportionally stronger than fresh — use about half the volume when substituting dried for fresh in a syrup recipe.
🫙
Thyme Simple Syrup
Combine equal parts sugar and water. Once sugar dissolves, remove from heat, add 6 to 8 fresh sprigs, and steep for 20 to 30 minutes, tasting regularly. Thyme is more forgiving than lavender — it takes longer to overstep into medicinal territory — but still benefits from frequent tasting. Strain, cool, and refrigerate.
Keeps for 3 to 4 weeks refrigerated. Lemon thyme syrup is made identically and produces a notably brighter, more citrus-forward result.
🧊
Syrup Ice Cubes
Freeze finished thyme syrup in ice cube trays for up to 6 months. Each cube is roughly 1 oz — enough for a single cocktail. Thyme and lemon thyme syrups are worth keeping as separate cube batches — they look identical but produce quite different drinks. Label everything clearly.
Drop a cube directly into the shaker with your other ingredients. It dilutes as it breaks down, so adjust your ice quantity slightly if using frozen cubes.
🍯
Thyme Honey
Gently warm honey (don't boil) and steep 4 to 5 fresh thyme sprigs for 20 to 30 minutes. The result is a rounded, herbal-floral sweetener that works beautifully in whiskey drinks, lemon-based cocktails, and anything that benefits from a honey note with herbal depth. Keeps for several months at room temperature.
Thyme honey and lemon juice is one of the simplest and most satisfying alcohol-free drink combinations from the bar garden. A spoonful in sparkling water is all it takes.
🫗
Spirit Infusion
Add 4 to 5 fresh sprigs to 250ml of gin or vodka. Thyme infuses more gently than rosemary — check every hour and expect to leave it for 2 to 4 hours for a well-developed infusion. Lemon thyme in gin is particularly good — the citrus note of the herb amplifies the gin's own citrus botanicals. Strain and use within 3 months.
Lemon thyme-infused gin is one of the most useful and distinctive spirit infusions from the bar garden. Try it in a gin and tonic or a simple spritz.
🌸
Pressed Flower Garnishes
Press flowering thyme sprigs in a flower press or between the pages of a heavy book for 2 to 3 weeks. Pressed thyme flowers are delicate, beautiful, and long-lasting as a bar garnish — lay one flat on the surface of a clear drink for an effect that's more botanical print than cocktail. A small store of pressed stems extends the garnish season well past summer.
Press the fullest, most symmetrical flowering stems. They should be fully open but not past their peak.

At the bar

Using Thyme in Drinks

Thyme is one of the most flexible herbs at the bar — it works as a background note, a supporting flavour, or occasionally a lead element. Here's how to get the most from both common and lemon thyme.

🫙
Simple Syrup
The most reliable way to extract and control thyme's flavour in a drink. Common thyme syrup has earthy, herbal depth. Lemon thyme syrup is brighter and more citrus-forward. Both can be used in the same way as any flavoured syrup — in shaken drinks, built cocktails, and mocktails. Start with half an ounce and adjust.
Make both common and lemon thyme syrups when the plant is flowering — the flavour is at its most complex and interesting during this period.
🌸
Flowering Garnish
A single flowering thyme sprig in a gin and tonic or a clear sparkling drink is one of the most beautiful garnishes in the bar garden — small, precise, and botanical in the best sense. It adds fragrance that carries with every sip. Harvest these on the day you plan to use them; they wilt within a few hours.
Choose sprigs where the flowers are just opening. A mix of buds and open flowers on the same sprig is the most visually interesting garnish.
🧉
Muddling
Thyme muddled well is clean and aromatic — the thin, soft stems don't contribute the bitterness that rosemary can. Use young tip growth, press gently 3 to 4 times, and strain. Works particularly well in gin and whiskey sours where you want a herbal background note that isn't as assertive as rosemary.
Strain carefully after muddling — thyme leaves are small and get through coarser strainers. A fine mesh tea strainer works better than a cocktail hawthorne strainer alone.
🍋
Citrus Partner
Thyme and lemon are one of the garden's great partnerships — earthy herb balanced against bright citrus, each making the other more interesting. Lemon thyme takes this further still, compressing both into one plant. Thyme lemonade, thyme and grapefruit soda, and thyme citrus syrup are all drinks built on this pairing.
Lemon thyme with lemon juice and sparkling water is the simplest alcohol-free bar garden drink. Try it before you add anything else — it may need nothing more.
🍯
Honey Combination
Thyme and honey have a natural affinity — thyme honey is a classic pairing in Mediterranean cooking and it translates beautifully to the bar. Replace plain honey syrup with thyme honey in any whiskey sour for an immediate upgrade. The honey softens the herb's earthiness; the herb deepens the honey's floral sweetness.
Thyme honey is good enough to serve by the spoonful in sparkling water with a squeeze of lemon. A remarkably good non-alcoholic drink that takes 30 minutes to make.
🫗
Spirit Infusion
Lemon thyme-infused gin is one of the bar garden's more underrated preparations. The citrus botanicals in gin and the citrus note of lemon thyme are complementary rather than redundant — the infusion produces something that tastes intentionally designed. Use in a simple gin and tonic or build into a thyme spritz with lemon and sparkling wine.
Taste every hour. Lemon thyme in gin takes longer than rosemary to develop — 3 to 4 hours is typical for a well-balanced infusion.

In the glass

What to Make with Garden Thyme

Thyme works best where you want herbal depth without dominance — it supports, extends, and deepens rather than leading. Start with the Garden Herb Gimlet and build from there.

Garden Herb Gimlet
Cocktail · On the site
Gin, fresh lime juice, herb syrup, and a sprig of garden thyme. The gimlet is one of the cleanest, most elegant classic cocktails — bright, tart, and assertive enough to carry herbal notes beautifully. Thyme's earthy-floral character lifts the gin without competing with the lime.
Spirit: London Dry Gin  ·  Herb: Common thyme or lemon thyme syrup
Thyme Gin & Tonic
Cocktail · Garden Classic
Add a quarter ounce of thyme syrup or lemon thyme syrup to a classic gin and tonic. The herbal note threads through the botanical gin and the bitter tonic in a way that makes the whole thing taste more considered. Garnish with a fresh thyme sprig or, when in season, a flowering stem for maximum effect.
Spirit: Floral or London Dry Gin  ·  Tonic: Light or classic tonic
Thyme Whiskey Sour
Cocktail · All Seasons
Bourbon or rye, fresh lemon juice, thyme honey syrup, and an optional foam. Thyme's earthiness works with aged whiskey in a similar way to rosemary — it adds herbal depth without competing with the spirit. Using thyme honey rather than plain syrup gives the drink a rounder, more complex sweetness.
Spirit: Bourbon or Rye  ·  Sweetener: Thyme honey syrup
Thyme Paloma
Cocktail · Tequila
Tequila blanco, fresh grapefruit juice, lemon thyme syrup, a squeeze of lime, and grapefruit soda over ice. Lemon thyme bridges the herbal and citrus elements in a way that common thyme can't quite manage — the citrus note in the herb ties together the grapefruit juice, the lime, and the tequila into something that tastes designed from the ground up.
Spirit: Tequila Blanco  ·  Herb: Lemon thyme specifically
Thyme Lemonade
Mocktail · Summer
Fresh lemon juice, thyme simple syrup, still or sparkling water, and ice. Adjust until the thyme is present but not leading — lemon should be the main voice with thyme adding complexity. A flowering thyme sprig as garnish makes this one of the most visually appealing garden mocktails of the whole year.
Use lemon thyme syrup for a more citrus-forward result; common thyme for a deeper, more herbal character
Thyme Honey Soda
Mocktail · Alcohol-Free
A spoonful of thyme honey dissolved in sparkling water with a squeeze of lemon and a few ice cubes. The simplest drink in the bar garden — and one of the most satisfying. The honey's floral sweetness and the thyme's earthy depth together with lemon's brightness produce a genuinely complex drink with three ingredients and no spirits required.
Garnish: Fresh thyme sprig  ·  Variation: Add a few drops of grapefruit bitters

Common Thyme Mistakes

Thyme is forgiving, but a few habits shorten its productive life or reduce what you get from it at the bar.

Mistake 01
Overwatering
The single most common thyme killer. Thyme is drought-tolerant and actively dislikes consistent moisture. Watering it on the same schedule as more water-hungry herbs — mint, basil — slowly rots the roots while the plant looks fine until it suddenly doesn't.
Fix: Water when the top inch of soil is dry. In containers, check the weight of the pot — if it feels light, it needs water. If it still feels heavy, it doesn't.
Mistake 02
Only growing one variety
Common thyme and lemon thyme are different enough to produce meaningfully different drinks. Growing only one means missing half of what thyme can do at the bar — particularly the bright, citrus-herb notes of lemon thyme in gin drinks and citrus cocktails.
Fix: Grow both in the same container or side by side in the border. Same care, twice the versatility.
Mistake 03
Missing the flowering window
Flowering thyme is one of the most beautiful bar garden garnishes of the year and the window is only a few weeks. Many people let the plant flower unnoticed and trim it back without using the blooms. The flowers are completely edible, fragrant, and visually striking in a glass.
Fix: Watch the plant as summer begins. When flower buds appear, harvest a few sprigs for the bar before cutting the plant back. It takes one day to make a drink you'll want to repeat every year.
Mistake 04
Keeping a plant too long
Thyme has a productive lifespan of 3 to 5 years. After that, most of the plant is woody and new tip growth is sparse. Many gardeners try to get more out of an old plant when a new cutting or a fresh plant would immediately produce more and better material for the bar.
Fix: Take cuttings every 2 to 3 years to keep young plants in rotation. Snip a few soft tip stems, root them in compost, and keep the best-performing plant going forward.
Mistake 05
Over-steeping the syrup
Thyme syrup is more forgiving than lavender, but it still becomes unpleasantly bitter if steeped for too long — particularly common thyme, which has a slightly more medicinal quality when over-extracted. Following recipe times without tasting is the mistake.
Fix: Steep off the heat, taste every 10 minutes, and stop when the flavour is clearly present but clean. Over-steeped thyme syrup makes even a good cocktail taste like a health tonic.
Mistake 06
Treating it as a kitchen herb only
Most people grow thyme for cooking. It takes a small mental shift to see it as a bar herb too — but once you've made a thyme honey syrup or had a gin and tonic with a flowering thyme garnish, the shift is permanent. The same plant, twice as useful.
Fix: The next time you walk past your thyme plant, cut a sprig for a drink rather than the pot. Everything else follows from there.
