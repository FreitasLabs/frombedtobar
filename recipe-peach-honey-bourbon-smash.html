Peach Honey Bourbon Smash

A ripe summer peach muddled with thyme-infused honey, bourbon, and fresh lemon. The drink that tastes like August — warm, sweet, and a little complex. Made from the orchard and the garden, five minutes before you sit down.

🍑 Peach 🌾 Thyme 🍯 Honey Bourbon Summer 5 min

The Recipe

Peach Honey Bourbon Smash

The cocktail version is built on bourbon; the mocktail uses strong chilled peach juice and tea for body. Both start with the same muddled peach and thyme honey base — the garden work is identical, the spirit is optional.

  • 2 oz bourbon (wheated style preferred)
  • 🍑 Half a ripe peach, pitted and quartered
  • ½ oz thyme honey syrup (recipe below)
  • ¾ oz fresh lemon juice
  • 🌾 1 fresh thyme sprig for muddling
  • Garnish: peach slice + thyme sprig
  • 🍑 Half a ripe peach, pitted and quartered
  • ½ oz thyme honey syrup
  • ¾ oz fresh lemon juice
  • 🌾 1 fresh thyme sprig for muddling
  • 1 oz fresh peach juice or strong brewed oolong tea, chilled
  • 2 oz sparkling water
  • Garnish: peach slice + thyme sprig
Add the quartered peach and one thyme sprig to the shaker. Muddle firmly until the peach is completely broken down — 6 to 8 good presses, turning the muddler each time. Add bourbon (or tea and peach juice for mocktail), thyme honey syrup, and fresh lemon juice. Fill with ice. Shake hard for 12–15 seconds — peach needs a vigorous shake to integrate properly. Double-strain through a hawthorne strainer and fine mesh strainer into a rocks glass over ice. Top mocktail version with sparkling water. Garnish with a fresh peach slice notched onto the rim and a thyme sprig pressed alongside it.

The Sweetener

Thyme Honey Syrup

Thyme honey is the quiet hero of this drink. The honey's floral sweetness rounds the bourbon's edges; the thyme adds an earthy, herbal note that lifts the peach rather than competing with it. See the Thyme Growing Guide for more on growing and using thyme at the bar.

Syrup Recipe
Thyme Honey Syrup
  • 1 cup honey (a mild, floral honey like clover or orange blossom works best)
  • 🌾 4–5 fresh thyme sprigs
  • ¼ cup hot water (to thin the honey to pourable syrup consistency)
Warm the honey gently in a small saucepan over low heat — don't boil, just warm enough to loosen it. Add the thyme sprigs, remove from heat, and steep for 20 to 30 minutes. Add the hot water, stir to combine, and strain out the thyme. Cool completely before bottling. For a thinner syrup (easier to measure and shake), add up to ½ cup water.
Keeps several months at room temperature, longer refrigerated · Plain honey syrup (1:1 honey and hot water) works as a substitute · Use in bourbon drinks, tea cocktails, and lemonade

In the Garden

Growing Thyme for the Bar

Thyme is one of the most useful herbs in the bar garden because it bridges the gap between summer fruit drinks and the aged spirit flavors of bourbon and whiskey. Read the full Thyme Growing Guide — varieties, harvest tips, drying, and every way to use it at the bar.

🌾
Thyme in Summer
Thyme is at its most fragrant in summer, when heat and sunlight concentrate the essential oils in the leaves. Harvest in the morning before the heat of the day dissipates the volatile compounds. For the honey infusion, use fresh thyme at peak harvest — the difference versus dried is noticeable in the finished syrup.
🍑
Choosing Peaches
Freestone peaches — where the flesh separates cleanly from the pit — are easiest for muddling. White peaches are sweeter and more delicate; yellow peaches are more acidic and robust. Either works in this drink. The key is ripeness: a fully ripe peach should yield to gentle pressure and smell strongly of peach at the stem end.
🍯
Honey from the Garden
If you're lucky enough to have access to local raw honey, this drink is the perfect showcase for it. Local honey carries the flavor of local flowers, which varies region to region and season to season. A mild clover honey lets the thyme and peach shine. A strongly flavored wildflower honey will assertively add its own character — which can be beautiful or overpowering depending on the batch.

Bartender Notes

Tips for getting it right

The Peach
Ripe is non-negotiable
An underripe peach produces a starchy, flavorless mash when muddled. A ripe peach produces juice. Press the peach with your thumb before you cut it — it should yield. Smell the stem end: a ripe peach smells like a peach. An underripe one smells like nothing. If your peaches aren't ripe, let them sit on the counter for another day or two before making this drink.
Bourbon
Go softer, not bolder
This drink benefits from a bourbon that complements the fruit rather than overpowering it. Wheated bourbons — Maker's Mark, Larceny, Weller — are softer and rounder, which lets the peach come through. High-rye bourbons (Buffalo Trace, Wild Turkey) are spicier and can compete with the fruit. Save the big, assertive bourbons for drinks where the spirit should lead.
Muddling
Muddle until there's nothing left
Unlike basil or mint, peach can take a vigorous muddling. Press firmly and thoroughly until you have a wet, pulpy mash with no large chunks remaining. This maximizes the juice extraction and ensures the flavor integrates fully when shaken. The double-strain will remove all solids, so you want to extract everything you can.
Balance
Taste the peach before measuring
Very sweet, ripe peaches need less honey syrup. A slightly tart peach (or one that's a day from peak) benefits from the full half ounce. The finished drink should taste like peach, bourbon, and lemon in roughly equal presence — none of the three should dominate the others completely. If it's too sweet, add a squeeze more lemon. If too tart, add a small pour of extra honey.

Serve It With

Food & Occasion Pairings

🍖
Food
Grilled pork tenderloin with peach glaze. Prosciutto with ripe melon and mint. Soft cheeses with honeycomb. Cornbread. Anything off a backyard grill in late July — the smoke of the grill and the smoke of the bourbon belong together.
🌇
Occasion
The after-work drink when it's still 85°F at 7 PM and the peaches from the market are in a bowl on the counter. Porch evenings, backyard dinners, slow late-summer weekends when nobody's in a hurry to do anything.
🍂
Best Time
July through early September. Peak peach season in most of the US runs 6 to 8 weeks in the heat of summer — that's the window. Make this drink as often as you can during those weeks, because when peach season is over, it's over until next year.
