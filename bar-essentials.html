For the Home Bartender

Build a bar worth drinking from.

You don't need a full liquor store or a professional kit. You need the right things — and to know how to use them. This is what actually matters behind a home bar.

Jump to: The Kit Base Spirits Techniques Ratios Bar Pantry

The Kit

Tools you actually need

A good home bar doesn't require much equipment. These are the things that make a real difference — buy them once and they last for years.

🥤
Buy this first
Cocktail Shaker
A standard cobbler or Boston shaker handles everything you'll make. The cobbler (three-piece with built-in strainer) is easier for beginners. Boston shakers are faster once you get comfortable.
💡 Don't shake too long — 12 to 15 seconds is enough. Over-shaking dilutes the drink.
📏
More important than you think
Jigger
Measuring is the single biggest difference between a good drink and a great one. A double-sided jigger (1oz / 2oz) covers almost every recipe. Free-pouring looks cool but costs consistency.
💡 Measure every time until the ratios feel natural. Most experienced bartenders still measure.
🌿
Essential for garden cocktails
Muddler
A wooden or stainless muddler is what unlocks fresh herbs, berries, and citrus. Press and twist — don't pound. You want to release oils, not shred the plant material into the drink.
💡 Muddle herbs gently. Mint especially — overmuddling releases bitter chlorophyll.
🧊
Underrated
Bar Strainer
A Hawthorne strainer fits over a Boston shaker. A fine mesh strainer (used together) catches herb bits and ice shards for a clean pour. Both together is called double-straining.
💡 Double-strain any drink with muddled herbs or egg white for a professional finish.
🥄
For stirred drinks
Bar Spoon
A long-handled bar spoon is for stirring spirit-forward drinks like a Negroni or Manhattan. Stir, don't shake — shaking adds air and cloudiness you don't want in those drinks.
💡 Stir for 30 seconds with ice. The goal is cold and diluted, not frothy.
🍋
Use it every time
Citrus Juicer
Fresh juice makes a bigger difference than almost any other ingredient swap. A simple handheld citrus press works perfectly. Bottled lemon and lime juice tastes flat and slightly off — skip it.
💡 Juice citrus at room temperature — you'll get significantly more juice than cold fruit.
🧊
Overlooked upgrade
Ice
Big ice melts slower and dilutes less. A large cube or sphere mold is a cheap upgrade that immediately improves spirit-forward drinks. Crushed ice is for juleps and swizzles — it dilutes fast by design.
💡 Use filtered water for clear ice. Tap water minerals make ice cloudy and can affect taste.
⚖️
Optional but useful
Kitchen Scale
Useful when making syrups in large batches. A 1:1 simple syrup is exactly that — equal weight of sugar and water. Measuring by weight is more accurate than volume for batch work.
💡 Once you make syrups by weight you won't go back. Consistency every time.
🔪
Every bar needs one
Paring Knife & Cutting Board
For garnishes, citrus twists, and slicing fruit. A sharp paring knife is safer and faster than a dull one. Keep a small dedicated cutting board at the bar so you're not running to the kitchen mid-drink.
💡 A proper citrus twist: cut a thin strip of peel, hold it over the glass, and squeeze to release the oils. Then run it around the rim.

The Bottles

Building your base spirits

You don't need every bottle. Start with two or three and learn them well. These six cover the vast majority of classic and garden cocktails — and you don't need to spend a fortune on any of them.

🌾
Vodka
Neutral · Versatile · Beginner-friendly

The most neutral base spirit — it gets out of the way and lets your garden ingredients shine. Perfect for herb-forward drinks, fruit smashes, and anything where the produce is the star.

Good starting bottles
  • Tito's Handmade — clean, affordable, widely available
  • Ketel One — smooth, slightly more character
  • Grey Goose — reliable if you want to spend more
🌿
Gin
Botanical · Herbaceous · Natural pairing

The most natural fit for a garden bar. Gin is already botanical — juniper, citrus peel, coriander — so fresh herbs and flowers feel at home in it. London Dry is classic; New Western styles are more floral and approachable.

Good starting bottles
  • Hendrick's — cucumber and rose, perfect for garden drinks
  • Tanqueray — classic London Dry, juniper-forward
  • The Botanist — complex, floral, excellent with herbs
🌵
Tequila Blanco
Bright · Earthy · Fruit-forward

Blanco tequila is unaged and bright — it pairs beautifully with citrus, watermelon, hibiscus, and spicy ingredients like jalapeño. Look for 100% agave on the label. Mixto tequilas (with added sugars) give you the headache people blame on tequila.

Good starting bottles
  • Espolòn Blanco — great value, clean agave flavor
  • Olmeca Altos — smooth, good for sipping and mixing
  • Patrón Silver — reliable, widely available
🎋
Bourbon
Warm · Oaky · Caramel notes

Bourbon's sweetness and vanilla notes pair naturally with stone fruits, honey, thyme, and rosemary. It's approachable, affordable, and makes the transition from cocktail to cool evening drink feel effortless.

Good starting bottles
  • Buffalo Trace — the benchmark, excellent value
  • Bulleit — higher rye content, slightly spicier
  • Woodford Reserve — smooth, great for sipping and mixing
🍬
White Rum
Light · Sweet · Tropical

Light rum is the base for mojitos, daiquiris, and anything with fresh fruit and mint. It's sweeter and softer than vodka, which makes it easy to drink but requires a little more acid to balance.

Good starting bottles
  • Bacardí Superior — the classic, does the job well
  • Plantation 3 Stars — more complex, slightly more character
  • Flor de Caña 4 — smooth, good quality for the price
🌸
Elderflower Liqueur
Floral · Light · Garden-forward

St-Germain is the standard. It's not a base spirit but it belongs in a garden bar — a small pour transforms almost any drink with floral, slightly honeyed sweetness. Works with gin, vodka, prosecco, and sparkling water.

Good starting bottles
  • St-Germain — the original, still the best
  • Elderflower tonic — non-alcoholic option, works similarly

How to Make Them

Techniques that actually matter

Most home bartender mistakes come down to a handful of technique issues. Get these right and everything else follows.

01
Shake vs. Stir — and when to use each
Shake drinks with citrus juice, egg white, cream, or any ingredient that needs to be integrated. Stir spirit-forward drinks (Negroni, Manhattan, Martini) where you want clarity and no air bubbles. When in doubt: if it has juice, shake it.
Rule: Citrus = shake. Spirits only = stir. Everything else = use your judgment.
02
Dilution is an ingredient
Shaking or stirring with ice doesn't just chill the drink — it adds water. A properly diluted cocktail is smoother and more balanced than an undiluted one. About 25% dilution is the target. That's roughly 12–15 seconds of shaking or 30 seconds of stirring.
Rule: Taste before you pour. If it tastes harsh or too strong, stir or shake a few seconds longer.
03
Balance: sweet, sour, strong, weak
Every good cocktail balances these four elements. Strong is the spirit. Sweet is syrup or liqueur. Sour is citrus. Weak is dilution or water. When a drink tastes off, figure out which element is out of balance — then fix that one thing.
Rule: Too strong → more dilution or citrus. Too sweet → more lemon or lime. Too sour → a touch more syrup.
04
The dry shake for foam
When a recipe calls for egg white or aquafaba, dry shake first — shake without ice for 15 seconds to build the foam protein structure. Then add ice and shake again. Skipping the dry shake gives you a flat, watery foam instead of a thick, stable head.
Rule: Dry shake first, wet shake second. Always.
05
Express a citrus peel — don't just drop it in
Cut a coin-sized piece of citrus peel. Hold it colored-side down over the glass, pinch sharply to spray the oils across the surface of the drink. Then run the peel around the rim. This step adds aroma that changes the entire drinking experience.
Rule: The nose is half the drink. A properly expressed peel is worth the extra 10 seconds.
06
Build in the glass when possible
Some drinks — gin and tonics, spritzes, highballs — are built directly in the glass over ice. Add spirits first, then modifiers, then carbonated ingredients last. Pour soda slowly down the side of the glass to preserve the bubbles.
Rule: Always add carbonation last. Always pour it slowly.

The Formulas

Ratios that always work

Once you know these ratios you can build almost any cocktail from scratch — with or without a recipe. They're the framework behind hundreds of classic drinks.

The Sour
2 : ¾ : ¾
Spirit, citrus, sweetener. The foundation of Margaritas, Daiquiris, Whiskey Sours, and Gimlets. Master this ratio and you can build a sour out of almost any spirit and fruit combination.
Example: 2 oz gin + ¾ oz lime + ¾ oz simple syrup = Gimlet
The Spritz
3 : 2 : 1
Sparkling wine, aperitivo (or syrup), soda water. Light, low-alcohol, endlessly adaptable. Swap the aperitivo for lavender syrup and you have a garden spritz.
Example: 3 oz prosecco + 2 oz Aperol + 1 oz soda = Aperol Spritz
The Old Fashioned
2 oz : 1 tsp : 2 dashes
Spirit, sugar, bitters. Three ingredients, stirred. The oldest cocktail formula there is — it works with bourbon, rye, rum, or even aged tequila. Swap the sugar for herb syrup to bring the garden in.
Example: 2 oz bourbon + 1 tsp thyme syrup + 2 dashes bitters = Garden Old Fashioned
The Highball
1 : 3 to 4
Spirit to mixer. Simple, refreshing, low-effort. The ratio of spirit to soda changes the strength. A 1:3 is boozy; a 1:4 is sessionable. Always build over ice and add soda last.
Example: 2 oz bourbon + 6 oz ginger beer over ice = simple and perfect
The Negroni
1 : 1 : 1
Equal parts spirit, sweet vermouth, bitter aperitivo. Stirred, not shaken. One of the most reliable formulas in bartending — the equal-parts approach makes it easy to batch for a crowd.
Example: 1 oz gin + 1 oz sweet vermouth + 1 oz Campari = Negroni
The Simple Syrup
1 : 1
Equal parts sugar and water by weight. Heat until dissolved, cool, bottle. A rich simple syrup is 2:1 sugar to water — sweeter and thicker, so you use less. All herb syrups start here.
Example: 200g sugar + 200g water + handful of mint = mint syrup

Stock the Bar

Your home bar pantry list

Beyond the spirits, these are the ingredients that fill the gaps and make cocktails taste complete. Most keep for a long time and work across dozens of recipes.

Acids & Citrus
  • Fresh lemons Buy weekly — use constantly
  • Fresh limes Buy weekly — essential for sours
  • Fresh grapefruit For Palomas and spritzes
  • Orange peel For garnish and Old Fashioneds
  • Citric acid powder Optional — extends sour flavor without adding liquid
Sweeteners
  • Simple syrup (1:1) Make a batch every 2 weeks
  • Rich simple syrup (2:1) Lasts longer, use half as much
  • Honey syrup 1:1 honey and warm water — lasts 2 weeks
  • Agave syrup Pairs naturally with tequila
  • Demerara syrup Darker sugar, great in Old Fashioneds
Bitters & Modifiers
  • Angostura bitters The essential — used in dozens of classics
  • Orange bitters Regan's or Fees — pairs with gin and bourbon
  • Dry vermouth Refrigerate after opening — use within 3 weeks
  • Sweet vermouth Refrigerate after opening — Cocchi or Carpano
  • Tonic water Buy quality — Fever-Tree or Q makes a difference
  • Sparkling water Keep several on hand — runs out fast

Drink responsibly. Every mocktail on this site is just as good as the cocktail version. Enjoy the garden. Enjoy the bar. Be safe, be kind, and look after each other.
